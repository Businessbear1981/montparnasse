## 3. Inventory of original creative work — NVAI platform

The following is a categorical inventory of work conceived, directed, and produced by Sean Gilmore (with AI-assisted code generation operating under his direction). Every item below pre-dates 29 May 2026.

### 3.1 Platform conception
- The concept of an **ultra-luxury cinematic immersive digital gallery** as a sales channel for fine art held under non-disclosure
- The brand name **Napa Valley Art Institut** (French spelling, no trailing 'e') and the production domain *napavalleyartinstitut.com*
- The brand mark and logo placement (top-left of the cinematic home page)
- The single-business model: cinematic gallery + direct sale of paintings + Kiki marquee wing + auction events
- The decision to base the visitor experience at a fictional **Villa Monticello** — a Tuscan-style French chateau in Napa Valley
- The selection and roster of approximately **twenty-eight paintings** from collections held by NVAI and partners (Modigliani, Picasso, Chagall, Monet, Matisse, Kandinsky, Raphael, Da Vinci, Pollock, Bernard, Kahlo, and the marquee Modigliani–Kiki canvas)

### 3.2 Spatial architecture
- Visitor journey: **Home aerial still → /entry slow cinematic zoom → /foyer → Staircase / Grand Hall / Garden Door**
- Upstairs landing with three doors: **Bernard chapel · Kandinsky's creepy room · Raphael studiolo**
- Grand Hall structured as a programme of the evening: **chateau ballroom → modern art party → digital curated auction house**
- Garden patio with three thresholds: **Parlor · Matisse pavilion · Grounds**
- Grounds artist wings: **Monet · Picasso · Da Vinci · Frida Kahlo**
- The Parlor as bohemian bar with the Chagall sub-room behind it
- The Cabinet de Curiosités (Modigliani) and the Pollock Studio inside the Grand Hall
- The DDNDA as an **optional document, never a gate** — visitors are not blocked from entry

### 3.3 Cinematic and visual direction
- The use of **5K cinematic backdrop video** as the spatial ground of every page
- The **400ms crossfade rotation** of inspiration videos within each wing
- The **slow-playback (0.35x – 0.6x)** policy for the home aerial and the entry zoom, to create the immersive cinematic register
- Period-appropriate visual treatments per wing (Bauhaus geometry for Kandinsky; Renaissance studiolo for Raphael; bohemian smoke for the Parlor; Mediterranean light for Matisse; etc.)
- Typography hierarchy: **Didot** for cinematic titles, **Garamond/Body** for prose, monospace for placards
- Colour palette: **gold (#C9A84C), ivory, midnight, oxblood** — restrained luxury, no neon, no holographic, no Web3 aesthetic

### 3.4 The twelve artist wings — narrative architecture
Each wing structured as a **multi-chapter biographical journey** with chapter-by-chapter backdrop video swaps, hero painting cards, and prose I authored or directed:
- Monet (4 chapters), Picasso (4 chapters across Bateau-Lavoir / Boisgeloup / Mougins), Chagall (3 chapters), Da Vinci (5 chapters), Frida Kahlo (4 chapters)

## 4. Inventory of original creative work — Montparnasse book project

All of the following are creative-direction calls made by Sean Gilmore on 29 May 2026, in real time, with AI-assisted prose drafting under his direction:

### 4.1 Title, format, genre
- The working title **Montparnasse** (single word, no subtitle)
- The genre classification as **narrative nonfiction biography — a literary biopic** with disclosed composite characters
- The comparable-title shelf positioning: *Just Kids* (Patti Smith), *The Devil in the White City* (Erik Larson), *Empire of Pain* (Patrick Radden Keefe)

### 4.2 Voice and tone
- The narrator's voice as **Richard Triberg in the first person**
- The five-layer voice stack:
  1. **Holden Caulfield** as the spine register
  2. **Hunter S. Thompson** as the late-night fuse
  3. **Indiana Jones / The Da Vinci Code** as the action shape
  4. **Hemingway** as a historical character (not a stylistic model)
  5. **Fitzgerald** as the period wallpaper for 1920s scenes

### 4.3 Structural architecture
- **The dual-timeline parallel structure** — every chapter holds Richard in the present and Kiki in the past
- **The fade-in / fade-out mechanism** triggered by specific verifiable facts about the canvas
- **The discipline ceiling**: nothing that could not actually happen — no conspiracy, no guns, real-world stakes only

### 4.4 The twelve-chapter spine
- **Prologue: Europe, 1918–1922** (Fitzgerald wallpaper, no Richard)
- **I. Bill** — the phone call that began the four years
- **II. The Red Pillow** — the 109-year-old shared-studio discovery (Modi + C.R.W. Nevinson)
- **III. Summer Rain** — the July 1917 night the painting was made
- **IV. For Richard — Find Her** — Bill's funeral and the package Bill's widow hands Richard the next morning with the handwritten note *For Richard — find her*
- **V. Bratislava** — the Slovak owners
- **VI. The Wildenstein Letter, 2012** — Marc was the curator then
- **VII. Alba** — the door opener at Restellini
- **VIII. Marc** — the face-to-face
- **IX. March 18, 2026 — The Private Jet** — the painting flies Prague → Le Bourget → Geneva
- **X. The Geneva Freeport** — the painting under particle physics
- **XI. Four Days in Montparnasse** — Richard walks the streets
- **XII. The Certificate** — Marc signs or does not sign; Camille calls

### 4.5 Characters originated by Sean Gilmore
- **Camille Aubry** — fictional senior scientist at Institut Restellini Geneva, the lab-side buddy / partner. Composite character. Fictional. Lives modern timeline only. Conceived 29 May 2026.
- The full character bible for Camille (temperament, training, role in the buddy-cop dynamic, her place in the final phone call)
- The dramatic treatment of **Bill** as the late mentor whose package is the inciting moment of Richard's modern arc
- The dramatic treatment of the **period film director** as the abusive antagonist of Kiki's 1916–1917 chapter (existence flagged by Richard verbally; dramatic placement and arc by Sean)

### 4.6 Plot beats originated by Sean Gilmore
- **Bill's package** — the brown paper parcel, the string, the handwritten note *For Richard — find her*, handed over by the widow the morning after a somber funeral
- **The summer rain night** in July 1917 — Modi watching Kiki from a corner of the Rotonde for two years, then a sudden rainstorm, the wine, the walk back to the studio on the rue de la Grande Chaumière, the pose, the crossed-hands gesture
- **The Madonna of Montparnasse framing** — Kiki positioned for the modern reader as the Madonna of her time, the Lady Gaga, the Elizabeth Taylor
- **The Camille phone call ending** — the book's last conversation is between Camille and Richard, not Marc's email
- The Kiki life arc — biographical chapter-by-chapter spine: Châtillon-sur-Seine, Paris childhood, modelling, the film director, the Modi obsession, Man Ray, the peak years, the memoir, the falling, the war, the hospital ward, March 1953

### 4.7 Thesis and emotional architecture
- The unstated thesis: *the wreckage was the same, but the connection was above the pain — and that is what we have lost*
- The parallel between Circa Montparnasse over-indulgence and modern over-indulgence (then: absinthe, opium, jazz, sex; now: Adderall, Instagram, OnlyFans, the dopamine economy)
- The historical backdrop weave (post-WWI Europe, industrial revolution, modernity in its infancy, the Lost Generation)

### 4.8 Disclosure architecture for the book
- The author's-note-on-sources front-matter convention
- The chapter-by-chapter sources appendix convention
- The composite-character disclosure for Camille Aubry
- The reconstructed-scene disclosure for the summer rain and Bill's package
