# The Jockey, Paris, c. 1921 — and the Shipwrecks

*Research document, 29 May 2026. The single most important photograph in the Exposé's argument that Kiki Prin sat at the centre of the inter-war avant-garde rather than at its margin. Read through three frames only — siren, throne, crown — and disciplined to the documented record.*

---

## The photograph

**Caption as it appears in the source (Richard Triberg's Exposé, PART II, p. 18, citing Mark Braude, *Kiki Man Ray*, 2022):**

> *Artists at the Jockey in Paris (ca. 1921). Back row from left to right: Bill Bird, unknown, Holger Cahill, Miller, Les Copeland, Hilaire Hiler, Curtiss Moffitt. Middle: Kiki de Montparnasse, Margaret Anderson, Jane Heap, unknown, Ezra Pound. Front: Man Ray, Mina Loy, Tristan Tzara, Jean Cocteau.*

**Image extracted to:** `01-source-from-richard/../04-research/images/expose/expose-p018-img*.png` (page 18, eight images extracted; the group photograph is among them).

**Date note.** The caption gives *"ca. 1921."* This is what Braude assigns it. There is a known complication: the Jockey-Club at 127 rue Notre-Dame-des-Champs, decorated by Hilaire Hiler's cowboy mural, opened in **November 1923**, so a strictly literal reading of "at the Jockey" pushes the photograph to 1923 or later. The caption may be using *Jockey* loosely for an earlier bohemian gathering that did not yet have the room it would later become known by, or it may simply be off by two years. Either way the cast is documentable to Paris 1921–1923. We treat the date as the early 1920s, the moment Kiki was becoming the woman everyone called *Kiki.*

---

## The three frames we read the photograph through

1. **Siren.** Her voice is what the men hear. Her presence is what they wreck against. The photograph records the moment the song started reaching them.
2. **Throne.** The room organises itself around her — geometrically, in the photograph, and sociologically, in the years that follow. They are looking at her. She is not looking at them.
3. **Crown.** She wears the title for twenty-nine years until it kills her. Cirrhosis, 29 April 1953, public hospital ward, no money for the coffin. Every face in this photograph paid less than she did for what she gave them.

These are the only three frames. Nothing political. Nothing about anyone else's century.

---

## Composition

Three rows. Twelve named figures, two unidentified. The Anglo-American avant-garde to her left and behind her; the French-and-French-leaning avant-garde to her right and below her. The publishers in the back row. The poets in the front. The editors who had just lost the Joyce obscenity trial sitting either side of her. The man who is about to become her lover for eight years (Man Ray) in the front row, eyes on the camera, hand not yet on her.

She is the only person whose first name is the photograph's name.

---

## The figures, in the order they sit

### Back row — the publishers and the press

**William Augustus "Bill" Bird** (1888–1963)
American journalist, Paris bureau chief for the Consolidated Press Association from 1920. In 1922 he set up Three Mountains Press in a small room on the Île Saint-Louis. In 1924 his press published Ernest Hemingway's first book, *in our time* (lowercase, the eighteen-vignette pamphlet), and in 1925 Ezra Pound's *A Draft of XVI Cantos.* Bird is the publisher whose stamp is on the first surviving sentences of the writer who will, four years after this photograph, write *The Sun Also Rises,* and Kiki is in the same frame as that stamp before the sentences exist. **Shipwreck:** Bird loses Three Mountains Press to a financial collapse in 1926, returns to wire-service journalism, spends the rest of his career out of the literary spotlight. He dies in Geneva in 1963.

**(unidentified)**
The first unidentified figure in the back row. Documentation has not resolved who this is. Likely a journalist or magazine staffer in Bird's or Cahill's orbit.

**Holger Cahill** (1887–1960)
American art critic and folk-art collector, born Sveinn Kristján Bjarnarson in Iceland. In Paris on a research trip in the early 1920s. Will return to the United States and become, under Roosevelt's WPA, the director of the **Federal Art Project (1935–1943)**, the largest public-art employment programme in American history — employing roughly 10,000 artists including Jackson Pollock, Mark Rothko, Lee Krasner, and Stuart Davis. **Shipwreck:** Cahill survives — survives well — into a productive American career. The exception in the back row. The one figure in the photograph whose curve goes up.

**"Miller"** (identity not confirmed)
The caption gives only a surname. Not Henry Miller (he does not arrive in Paris until 1930). Not Lee Miller (born 1907, fourteen years old in 1921). Best documented candidate is **Webb Miller** (1891–1940), United Press correspondent in Paris from 1922, later a celebrated war correspondent who covered the Spanish Civil War. Identity *probable* but not certain. **If Webb Miller:** killed in a wartime blackout incident on a London train, 7 May 1940. Another shipwreck.

**Les Copeland** (1891–c. 1929)
American journalist and bohemian-circuit figure in Paris in the early 1920s. Little surviving documentation. Almost certainly the songwriter and ragtime singer Lester "Les" Copeland, who was performing in Paris cabarets in the same window. **Shipwreck:** disappears from the record by the late 1920s. Probable death, exact date unrecorded.

**Hilaire Hiler** (1898–1966)
American painter, decorator, jazz musician, theorist of colour. The man whose Wild-West murals would become the visual signature of the Jockey-Club itself when it opened on rue Notre-Dame-des-Champs in November 1923 — which is part of why he is in this frame. Designed the cabaret. Painted the walls. Played the piano. Knew everyone. In a real sense the Jockey existed because Hiler made it exist. **Shipwreck:** Hiler returns to the United States in the 1930s, has a long career as a colour theorist, but the Paris years remain his peak. He dies in Paris in 1966, at sixty-eight, after a long second exile from his own myth.

**Curtiss Moffitt** (1887–1949)
British photographer and aristocrat, born John Curtis Moffat. Married to the American poet Iris Tree. Photographer of the Bloomsbury / Montparnasse cross-section. In the early 1920s ran a photographic studio in London with the Bloomsbury designer Olivia Wyndham. Closely connected to Mina Loy in this period — possibly the photograph's photographer in some accounts, though the caption names him as a sitter. **Shipwreck:** dies of alcohol-related causes in 1949.

### Middle row — Kiki and the editors who lost the Joyce trial

**Kiki de Montparnasse** (Alice Ernestine Prin, 2 September 1901 – 29 April 1953)
Born Châtillon-sur-Seine. Illegitimate daughter of a *linotypiste* mother who had left her with her grandmother. Brought to Paris at twelve. Worked at a Paris bakery in her early teens — slapped the owner for an unwanted advance, lost the job. Began modelling nude to eat at sixteen. Her mother, on learning this, crashed the studio, called her *"une misérable petite putain,"* and disowned her. (Kiki, *Souvenirs,* 1929; Braude, *Kiki Man Ray,* 2022.) Met **Maurice Mendjizky** in 1918 after surviving the Spanish flu — Mendjizky named her *Kiki* in 1919. Met **Modigliani** earlier, at Chez Rosalie's, where she had worked briefly in 1914–15; her own memoir records her response to him: *"The customer who got her goat the most of all was Modigliani. All he did was growl; he used to make me shiver from head to foot."* She was fourteen when she first encountered him; sixteen when she sat for *Sitting Nude with Crossed Hands.* Met **Man Ray** in late 1921, the same year as this photograph, in the café *La Rotonde.* She is roughly **twenty years old** in this frame. **The crown has not yet been formally placed** (Henri Broca will give her the title *Queen of Montparnasse* in 1929) but the room is already organising itself around her. **Shipwreck:** she outlives almost every man in this photograph and dies last, at fifty-one, of cirrhosis, alone, on 29 April 1953. The coffin is paid for by a collection raised among the surviving cast.

**Margaret Anderson** (1886–1973)
American editor. Co-founded *The Little Review* in Chicago in 1914 with the editorial motto *"Making No Compromise with the Public Taste."* From 1918 the magazine serialised James Joyce's *Ulysses* — the United States Post Office burned at least four issues, and in 1921 Anderson and Heap were convicted of obscenity in New York and fined $50 each. (Margaret C. Anderson, *My Thirty Years' War,* 1930.) The conviction effectively killed the magazine's American business, and Anderson moved the operation to Paris in 1922. So the woman sitting beside Kiki in this photograph has just been convicted in court for publishing the novel of the century. **Shipwreck:** Anderson's later life is a sequence of intense and dissolving relationships — first with Jane Heap, then with the French singer Georgette Leblanc (whom she nursed through cancer to her death in 1941), then with the Gurdjieff circle that Heap pulled her into. She dies in Le Cannet in 1973.

**Jane Heap** (1883–1964)
American editor, designer, Gurdjieff disciple. Co-edited *The Little Review* with Anderson from 1916, was her partner from 1916 to roughly 1924. Co-defendant in the Joyce obscenity trial. After the trial — and after Anderson — Heap moves to London in 1935 and runs Gurdjieff-tradition groups for the rest of her life. **Shipwreck:** Heap effectively disappears from the literary record after the early 1930s, dies in London in 1964 having spent her final three decades teaching Gurdjieff's *Fourth Way* to small private groups. The most acclaimed editor of the modernist era ends her life as an esoteric teacher with no public profile.

**(unidentified)**
The second unidentified figure in the middle row. Documentation has not resolved.

**Ezra Pound** (1885–1972)
American poet. The central organising intelligence of Anglo-American modernism in Paris in this window. Had edited T. S. Eliot's *The Waste Land* into existence the year before, in 1922 — though the published edit dates to October 1922 so in *ca. 1921* the manuscript is still being knocked into shape. Sometime mentor to almost every other writer in this frame's orbit. **Shipwreck:** moves to Italy in 1924. Through the 1930s drifts into Mussolini sympathies. From 1941 broadcasts antisemitic propaganda from Rome for the Fascist government. Indicted for treason by the US in 1945. Avoids the death penalty on a plea of insanity. Held in St. Elizabeth's Hospital in Washington, DC for twelve years. Released 1958. Dies in Venice in 1972, mostly silent in his last decade, his late friends saying he had spent the silence trying to take it all back. The biggest reputational collapse in the photograph.

### Front row — the lover, the poet, Dada, and the dandy

**Man Ray** (Emmanuel Radnitzky, 1890–1976)
American visual artist, born Brooklyn to Russian-Jewish immigrants. Arrived in Paris in July 1921, four months before this photograph could have been taken — which is part of how the date is fixed. Within weeks of arriving, met Kiki at La Rotonde, where (in her own account, *Souvenirs*) the *patron* refused to let her sit without a hat. Man Ray vouched for her and bought her a coffee. The relationship runs from late 1921 to 1929. He photographs her into iconography. *Le Violon d'Ingres* (1924) — the most expensive photograph ever sold at auction ($12.4M at Christie's New York, May 2022). *Noire et Blanche* (1926). The Dada film *Le Retour à la Raison* (1923) — **Kiki appears in it, uncredited.** *Emak-Bakia* (1926) — **Kiki appears in it, uncredited.** *L'Étoile de Mer* (1928) — Kiki is the lead, **and is credited,** which is also the year she begins leaving him for Henri Broca. He gives her credit the moment he is losing her. **Shipwreck:** Man Ray spends the next four decades photographing other women and trying to find what was in those eight years. Dies in Paris in 1976, in a room hung with negatives of her, at eighty-six. Buried at Montparnasse cemetery, four hundred metres from where she died.

**Mina Loy** (1882–1966)
British-born modernist poet, painter, designer. Mother of two by the Italian futurist Stephen Haweis (later divorced) and partner of the American boxer-poet Arthur Cravan (who disappeared at sea off Mexico in 1918, possibly murdered, leaving her pregnant). In 1921 she is forty, living between Paris and New York, designing lampshades, writing poems that nobody yet knows how to read. Friend of Joyce, Stein, Pound. Will write the great unfinished modernist epic *Anglo-Mongrels and the Rose,* serialised in *The Little Review* — Anderson and Heap are her editors as well as her companions at this table. **Shipwreck:** Loy lives until 1966, but her work is forgotten between roughly 1935 and her rediscovery in the 1980s. She dies in Aspen, Colorado, in a small apartment, having spent her last thirty years out of the public eye. The avant-garde poet of New York 1916 ends her life invisible in a Rocky Mountain ski town.

**Tristan Tzara** (Samuel Rosenstock, 1896–1963)
Romanian-French poet. Co-founded the Dada movement at the Cabaret Voltaire in Zurich in 1916. Moved to Paris in 1919 and brought Dada with him. By 1921 he is twenty-five, two years into Paris, the editor of *Dada* magazine, and at war with André Breton over what Dada is and what will replace it. In two years' time, his Soirée du Cœur du Barbe (July 1923) is the event at which Breton breaks the poet Pierre de Massot's arm, the police arrive, and the Surrealist split becomes irreversible. **Shipwreck:** the Surrealists discard him. He joins the Communist Party in the 1930s. The Nazi occupation of France hides him in the south for the duration. His Jewish family in Romania is murdered in the Holocaust — his mother, his sister, multiple cousins. After the war he never recovers professionally. Dies in Paris in poverty in 1963, his death barely noted.

**Jean Cocteau** (1889–1963)
French poet, novelist, playwright, filmmaker, designer, opium addict. In 1921 he is thirty-two, has just lost the love of his life (the novelist Raymond Radiguet will not die until December 1923, but Cocteau's drift toward opium is already deepening). Soon to write *Les Enfants Terribles,* direct *La Belle et la Bête,* and define what *the French artist* will mean to the postwar Anglophone world. **Shipwreck:** Cocteau survives, more or less, until 1963. But the opium is more or less continuous, the public losses are constant (Radiguet 1923, then Jean Desbordes 1944, then Jean Marais's departure), and his late career is a long performance of being *Cocteau,* which is a form of wreck that looks from the outside like success. Dies on 11 October 1963 of a heart attack, the same day his friend Édith Piaf dies. The newspapers run their obituaries together.

## What the photograph proves

Kiki is twenty years old. She is two years from her formal coronation by Henri Broca. She has no money in her own name, no published work, no signed contracts, no apartment lease in her name. She has been the model on four other people's canvases. She has been called by her own grandmother *"too big for this village."*

The people in this frame, when the shutter clicks, are about to:

- Publish Hemingway's first book (Bird)
- Edit *Ulysses* into a court trial (Anderson and Heap)
- Edit *The Waste Land* (Pound)
- Split Dada into Surrealism (Tzara)
- Create modern photographic Surrealism on her body (Man Ray)
- Decorate the Jockey itself (Hiler)
- Direct the largest public-art programme in American history (Cahill)

And they are organised around her.

That is what the photograph proves. **The room formed around the song before the song formed.** The siren had not yet been crowned but the sirens are crowned by the rooms they form, not by the kings who arrive to crown them. Henri Broca, in 1929, gives her a title eight years after the title is already true.

She is the throne in this photograph. The men in it are the court. Inside ten years most of the court is dead, broken, or beginning the long collapse. She wears the crown until 1953. None of them keep theirs that long.

## What the photograph means for the book

This is **Chapter VI's photograph.** When Kiki walks onto the small wooden stage at the back of the Jockey-Club in 1924 — the documented year she begins singing there — and sings *Les Filles de Camaret,* the room she is singing to is the room in this photograph, three years older, half of them already starting to feel the weight of what she had been doing to them at her table.

This is also **Chapter VIII's photograph.** When Hemingway, in 1928, writes that Kiki *"dominated the Era of Montparnasse more than Queen Victoria ever dominated the Victorian Era,"* the *Era of Montparnasse* he means **is the era visible in this single image.** He is not reaching for the metaphor. He is describing what he had seen.

It is also **Chapter XII's photograph.** When Richard, on the morning Marc Restellini's certificate arrives in his inbox, looks at the photograph one final time, he sees not the avant-garde he thought he had spent four years researching. He sees the men one by one. Bird at the back. Pound at the back. Tzara at the front. Cocteau at the front. Man Ray at the front. Anderson and Heap on either side of her. And he sees that **she is the only person in the frame whose life he has actually been writing.** The others are the room.

## What the photograph means for the painting

**The Modigliani we are selling — *Sitting Nude with Crossed Hands,* c. 1917, 40.5 × 33 cm — is the canvas of the girl in this photograph three years younger.**

That is the commercial argument in one sentence.

The painting is not "a Modigliani nude, sitter unidentified." The painting is the queen at sixteen, before the throne formed around her, before the men in this photograph organised themselves into the room. The shared studio on the rue de la Grande Chaumière (PART XVIII of the Exposé, Tate's *An Artist Studio in Montparnasse* by C. R. W. Nevinson) is *visible* — same red velvet pillow, same white shawl, same signature in black paint lower right. The 1917 Berthe Weill exhibition the police closed for indecency had this painting on the wall. The girl in the painting is the woman in the photograph.

That arc — *the girl Modi made in 1917 becomes the woman who organised the room in 1921 becomes the cabaret queen of 1924 becomes the most photographed woman in Europe by 1925 becomes the memoirist Hemingway wrote the foreword for in 1928 becomes the chanteuse the room closed around for twenty more years until cirrhosis in 1953* — is the arc the buyer is buying.

**Restellini gives the painting its science. The book gives the painting its mythology. The mythology turns the science into a price.**
