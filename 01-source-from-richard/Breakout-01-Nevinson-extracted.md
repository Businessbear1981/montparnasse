Chapter 7 -  The Lost Studio of Montparnasse 
The painting had hung in the Tate Gallery for nearly a century, silent and unassuming. No crowds gathered around it. No art 
historians clambered to decode its meaning. It was simply there—An Artist Studio in Montparnasse, by British painter Christopher 
Richard Wynne Nevinson. 
But beneath the layers of oil and pigment, a secret had been hiding. A secret so profound that, if true, it would rewrite a piece of 
art history. It took over 2,500 hours of relentless research—scouring archives, cross-referencing sketches, and unearthing 
forgotten letters—to uncover the truth. 
And the truth? 
This wasn’t just a painting of an artist’s studio. It was a painting of the studio—the Montparnasse studio shared by Nevinson and 
Amedeo Modigliani. 
A scandalous realization. Because if that was Modigliani in the painting… then the nude figure standing by the window could only 
be Alice Prin—better known as "Kiki de Montparnasse"  a title given to Alice in 1922 at the age of 22, under the tutelage of Man 
Ray, who she had a stormy decade long affair.
The greatest muse of the Parisian avant-garde. 
The pieces had been hiding in plain sight. A red velvet pillow, seen in multiple photographs.   A white shawl draped over a chair, a 
signature from Modigliani’s 1917 nude portraits, appearing in dozens of his works. And a forgotten letter, buried in the pages of 
history, accusing Nevinson of altering the painting before it was presented to Tate. 
But why? 
Why would Nevinson try to erase the past? 
A Shared Studio, A Hidden Past 
The world remembers Nevinson as a bold painter, a modernist who captured the grit of war and industry. But few knew that, for 
two years, he had shared a studio with Modigliani in the heart of Montparnasse. 
The revelation came like a shock-wave. A seated figure in the painting—long assumed to be just another artist—bore a haunting 
resemblance to Modigliani. The angular jawline, the placement of the ear against the thick beard, the way he slouched, lost in his 
work. 
Modigliani had lived in Montparnasse at a time when the world was against him. He was poor, drinking too much, his health 
failing. But he was also a genius, producing work at a feverish pace. And next to him?  Nevinson. 
Watching. Sketching. Absorbing. 
It had been so obvious, yet so overlooked. 
But there was one detail in the painting that held the key to it all. 
The nude figure by the window. 
Alice Prin - The Muse Who Owned Paris 
Alice Prin was thrown out of her mother’s home at twelve years old. Alone in Paris, she survived on the streets, taking any job, 
she could—until an artist found her and put a pencil to paper. 
By 1922, she was legendary.  Officially crowned  KIKI, "Queen of Montparnasse" in 1929 in a lavish ceremony. A muse to the 
greatest artists of her time—Man Ray, Fujita, Kisling, Calder, Hemingway and perhaps most famously….Modigliani.

It was no secret that Modigliani adored her. He sketched her obsessively. He saw something in her that no one else did—
something raw, untamed, almost electric. 
And so when the research team examined Nevinson’s painting, there she was. The same bobbed hair. The same posture.  
manner of her arms.  The same red velvet pillow seen in multiple photos and under Alice  when Modigliani painted her in 1917. 
And yet… when Nevinson’s painting was first placed on view at the Tate Gallery in 1927, the controversy erupted. 
Sisley Huddleston, a well-connected journalist, wrote a scathing letter to the New York Herald Tribune. He claimed Nevinson had 
added a nude figure to the painting that hadn’t been in the original sketch. But research proved otherwise—the nude had 
always been there. 
Huddleston wasn’t upset that a nude had been added. He was upset because he knew exactly who she was. 
Alice Prin
The girl who had once been a street urchin. The girl who had grown into the untouchable icon of Montparnasse. The girl whose 
image, immortalized in Nevinson’s painting, linked her to Modigliani in a way no one had ever realized before. 
The Secret Edits That Changed History 
But something happened between the painting’s first exhibition in 1926 and its arrival at Tate in 1927. Nevinson changed it. 
•
He removed the bearded artist, who else could it be but for Modigliani
•
He looped up the curtains, revealing more of the windows
•
He altered the basic beige fabric of the curtains in favor of heavy dark velvet, likely red
•
Curtains that originally hung straight to show windows continuing on either side
•
Which version was accurate? - likely the velvet - depicted in many of his nudes
•
He added the book on the left-hand edge of the table - a distraction, an attempt to shift focus
Why? 
The answer, researchers believe, is simple - fear. 
Modigliani’s reputation was already infamous. His name was tied to scandal, to wild nights and tragic endings. But Alice? She 
was something else entirely. 
She was still alive. Still famous. Still a fire-brand. At the peak of her stardom.  Crowned KIKI, Queen of Montparnasse in 1929 in a 
lavish ceremony.
And in 1926, a painting of Modigliani, sitting in a studio next to a teenage Alice, would have raised too many questions. Perhaps 
Nevinson worried that people would recognize the girl in the painting. Perhaps he feared the Leicester Galleries would refuse 
to continue to show it. Perhaps he simply didn’t want to be caught in the storm that was sure to follow. 
So, he did what any artist under pressure might do. 
He edited history.

There was one last detail that sealed the case. A March 3, 1926, issue of The 
Sketch Newspaper contained a color reproduction of the painting before it 
was altered. In it, the nude figure is not standing by the window—but 
reclining on the couch. 
Exactly as Sisley Huddleston had originally claimed. 
Nevinson had removed the artist. He had adjusted the composition. But he 
had never been able to erase the truth. 
And now, nearly a century later, the truth was out.  An Artist Studio in 
Montparnasse was never just an ordinary painting. 
It was a hidden portrait of Amedeo Modigliani and Alice Prin, frozen in 
time, locked away in the Tate Gallery for decades, waiting for someone 
to see what had been there, all along

In the interwar years, in burgeoning Montparnasse, artists searched relentlessly to capture the 
image reflected by the human face.  Each in his own fashion sought to reconstruct, in compelling 
portraits, human representation, Matisse and Chagall set their paintings ablaze with pure color, 
Soutine deployed naked expressiveness, Modigliani mastered the nude, while Picasso chose the 
road of deconstruction. The creative individuality inspired freedom, a freedom which was to imbue 
Montparnasse with a unique atmosphere and astound the visitors who flocked from the world over 
to discover its magic.

LOT ESSAY: 
Dating from 1925, the present work depicts Parisian café society and was painted during Nevinson's most productive period. It 
was painted a year after Sunday evening, punts of the Thames at Henley (sold in these rooms, 30 November 2000, lot 35, for a 
world record price, £201,750, private collection) and both paintings display elements of Nevinson's return to pre-war post-
impressionism. 
Although after the war, Nevinson had declared that he had given up futurism, Sur la Terrasse, Montparnasse is indebted to 
Nevinson's involvement with the Futurist movement, displayed in the striking diagonals shooting beyond the electric lights 
depicted. 'Electric lights recur frequently in Nevinson's work, as they did in the work of many of the Futurists; Nevinson recalls in 
Paint and Prejudice the ﬁrst time he saw a light bulb when staying at a hotel in Paris with his mother before the War. Visually, he 
was clearly as much interested in what they hid as what they revealed, thereby equating them with a complex set of values' (R. 
Ingleby (et. al.), loc. cit). 
The present work is readable across the picture plane, however, there is a fragmentation of the subject matter that is typical of 
Nevinson's compositions. The clustering confusion of heads that are crammed into the conﬁnes of the canvas convey to the 
viewer the cacophony of noise that must have risen from these outdoor cafés. The sharp diagonals descending from the lights 
are echoed in the roofs of the buildings and brought forward into the lower half of the painting, holding the crowd of people in a 
snapshot of stillness within their mass of sociableness.

Sisley Huddleston (1883–1952) was an English writer who adopted France as his home. Posted to Paris during the First 
World War, Huddleston became an expert in French politics and history. He also found time to socialise with and write 
about the literary and artistic expatriates of Paris, including Ford and Bowen. In fact, he was so often to be found at the 
famous English-language bookshop, “Shakespeare and Co.” that he was dubbed “Mr. Shakespeare”. 
Huddleston was ambivalent about the social and political changes he witnessed in his beloved France after the First World

Sisley Huddleston (28 May 1883 – 14 July 1952) was a British journalist and writer. After editing a British forces newspaper 
in the First World War, he was resident in Paris after the war until the 1930’s, writing for The Times (London) and the Christian 
Science Monitor.  In his Europe in Zigzags (1929) he supported the Pan-Europe manifesto of 
Richard von Coudenhove-Kalergi.[1] War Unless (1933) was a "deliberately alarmist" [2] call for revision of the Treaty of Versailles.
During the Second World War, he was in Vichy France, taking French citizenship and writing in sympathy with the regime.[3] He 
interviewed Marshal Philippe Pétain.  He was arrested in October 1944 by French authorities on treason charges.[4]  He was 
imprisoned by the Free French, in 1944 as a Vichy collaborator.[5] He wrote a number of works that were critical in particular of 
the Allied handling of the Liberation of France and politicians' diplomacy.
