Privatization & Restitution Laws 
The Czech privatization plan has been controversial. Essentially, it is a three-step process. "The first step was the re- 
privatization [restitution] of shops and enterprises to their original owners; the second step was the privatization of small 
industries; and the final step involved selling or breaking up the large state conglomerates”. The supporters of this three- step 
process envisioned a rapid privatization process that would radically transform the Czech economy similar to the Polish "shock 
treatment economics" program in 1989. As one top governmental official remarked, "You can't understand the scope of the 
challenge and opportunity until you realize that here, we don't own anything other than our toothbrushes”. Three main 
restitution laws were enacted in the years immediately following the Velvet Revolution. These laws are as follows: 
1.
The Small Restitution Law, "Act on Relieving the Consequences of Certain Property Injuries", and 
2.
The Large Restitution Law, "Extra-Judicial Act", and 
3.
The Land Law, "Adjustment of Ownership Rights of Land and Other Agricultural Property” 
Beginning in 1989 the speed of the privatization process depended upon how issues involving restitution were handled. 
Czechoslovakia was the first former communist country to enact such a sweeping law on restitution of property that was 
either confiscated or nationalized by the former communist regime. If the Czech Republic proves its sincerity about 
upholding property rights, foreigners may be encouraged to invest in or buy the remaining land and industry. Foreign 
investment is necessary to speed up the economic growth of this poor, formerly communist country. This began and 
continues to progress today.

Art Conservation and Art History in Czechoslovakia (1918 – 1989) 
On October 28, 1918, in the aftermath of the First World War, Czechoslovakia became a modern nation-state and enjoyed 
twenty years of relative peace and prosperity. In 1938, in the face of Nazi expansion, Chamberlain, Daladier, Hitler, and 
Mussolini met in Munich and formulated the infamous Munich Agreement. This agreement allowed Hitler to annex the German 
speaking Sudetenland region of Czechoslovakia. Soon thereafter, the French and British informed Czechoslovakia that if they 
acted alone in resisting Hitler, such resistance would be considered a provocation of war. Czechoslovakia remained compliant, 
and less than six months after the Munich agreement, Nazi troops marched on Prague. 
In February 1945 before the end of the Second World War in Europe (VE-Day, May 8, 1945), the leaders of the United States, 
Great Britain, France, and the Soviet Union met in Yalta to determine the fate of postwar Europe. Czechoslovakia wasone of the 
dividing points. Theleadersdecided that the Soviets would liberate Prague; consequently, Czechoslovakia fell under the Soviet 
sphere of influence, which ultimately led to the 1948 communist takeover of Czechoslovakia. From 1945 to 1948, the 
Czechoslovak (or Benes) government, acting within the scope of the 1920 Czechoslovak constitution, confiscated much of the 
land of the Sudeten Germans and Hungarian minority, who had collaborated or sympathized with the Nazis during the Second 
World War. Many of the large industries and businesses also were nationalized during these years. 
In February 1948, Soviet-backed communists took over Czechoslovakia and started the mass confiscation of private 
property and nationalization of industry. The final result of the Communist takeover was that Czechoslovakia endured the 
most intensive and comprehensive private property confiscation and business and industry nationalization of any of the 
Soviet bloc countries, excluding the Soviet Union. The communist government gave little or no consideration for confiscated 
property. 
Seizures were often carried out illegally, even under the laws passed by the communists. Owners of factories or farms were 
expelled from their houses on short notice, and sometimes sentenced at summary trials to hard labor, apparently for the sole 
crime of belonging to the “enemy class”. Many of the other former Soviet bloc countries were able to maintain some level of 
private property and small businesses, whereas not even "mom-and-pop shops" remained in Czechoslovakia. This extensive 
property and industry nationalization put Czechoslovakia at a severe disadvantage for reform in comparison to other former 
Soviet-bloc countries that broke free from communism in the revolutions of 1989. 
In her book, Mary Heimann, calls Czechoslovakia "…the state that failed…" It lasted for just seventy-four years during which it 
"…was federalized, centralized, dissolved and reconstituted…"; it experienced democracy, Fascism, Nazi occupation, 
Communism, Stalinism, Soviet occupation, Perestroika and democracy again. Situated right in the middle of Europe, this "… 
insecure zone in between Russia and Germany…" that "…does not trust politics or history…" jointly formed the cultural and 
intellectual milieu of what Kundera (1983) calls "… 
European cultural legacy…". As Heimann states, "This was a place where it was not only possible, but normal, for a single 
person to have all the varied experiences of being born a subject of the Habsburg Empire; brought up with the ideals of 
Wilsonian democracy; come of age under Nazism; joined in the postwar quest for social equality; being indoctrinated with 
Stalinism; successively re-educated in the ways of reform and neo- conservative Communism; converted to free-market 
Capitalism; and, all along, stubbornly clung to old-fashioned, Romantic nationalism." 
After the Velvet Revolution in November 1989, the first freely elected government of Czechoslovakia decided that to 
implement a free-market economy it was necessary to quickly transfer state-owned properties and enterprises into private 
hands. The newly elected political reformers believed that a free-market economy was based on private ownership of property, 
and they promptly began to explore means by which property could be shifted from state to private ownership. The new 
government saw the restitution of property to its original owners as a means not only to speed up the privatization process, 
but also a prerequisite for developing a free-market economy.' 
However, after implementing restitution laws, the government realized that these laws had quite the opposite effect. 
Although restitution laws initially slowed economic reform and privatization, they in fact created a sense that some justice 
had been achieved to compensate for the inexorable property confiscations and nationalization programs implemented by 
the communist Czechoslovak government. The restitution laws also reintroduced the Czechs to concepts of private 
ownership and other market-oriented legal principles and practices had quite the opposite effect.

Almost all of his official 22 nudes seem to have been done from 1916 through 1919. His style seemed to vary from the 
Reclining Nude with Blue Cushion inspired by Giorgione’s Sleeping Venus to his Reclining Nude inspired by Goya’s La Maja 
Desnuda and to his Girl in White Chemise where he shows facial, shoulder, arm, torso, and neck style changes. 
The attached examples show the way Modigliani could change styles based on what he saw and how he thought about the 
models. In easier nudes like #1 attached he wanted (according to curators more qualified than me) to reconcile his colors of 
the composition and the physical type with his lover Beatrice Hastings (1916). But then later (1917) he struck the style of #2 
where he made a substantial difference. Rather than the elegance and sophistication Modigliani used in #1 from the 
influence of Beatrice, number 2 by contrast is a plain looking, plump, dumpy body of a Courbet type to improve on the facts 
of nature. Modigliani still uses the firm, warm, illumination, simplified modeling. This is more of the Italian tradition of the 
full, ripe, human figure of his own Renaissance views. 
When an observer looks at the styles Modigliani used in creating his caryatids (earlier forms of nudes) and then later in his 
huge number of drawings, you will find in the illustrations the lines of the shoulders, arms, torso, thighs, and legs of very 
similar construction. Several of the drawings attached show the similar positioning of the legs and the folded arms as they 
appear in this painting. Review numbers 3,4,5 to see this comparison. There are also drawings of dark long hair with bobs 
and curving backs and heads turned sideways. It seems Modigliani favored his nude model’s heads turned to the left or right 
or tilted slightly off a direct gaze. Even his drawing show this tendency or style. 
Modigliani was obsessed with the human body. His friend Lipchitz once explained “He could never forget his interest in 
people, and he painted them, so to say, with abandon, urged on by the intensity of his feeling and vision” As the writer Jean 
Cocteau wrote “He reduced us all to his type, to the vision within, and he usually preferred to paint faces conforming to the 
physiognomy he required.” For Modigliani’s portraits, even his self-portraits, are not the reflection of his external 
observation, but of his internal vision. When studying the life of Alice Prin (Kiki Montparnasse) it seems he painted her as 
described by other observers as being “fleshy and chubby” at 17-19 years of age. Later renditions of her by Moise Kisling in 
the 1920’s after the death of Modigliani and her book titled “Memoirs of Kiki Montparnasse” show her own transitions. 
Modigliani was partial to sharp angles in the backgrounds through positioning of the figure. Upon detailed review, a 
Modigliani painting may reveal a chair, or table and a wall that is divided. Those appear in this painting. Modigliani would 
show wainscoting, rather than a draping sheet or elongated cushion as other models used, this model is sitting on a large, 
tasseled pillow. In an archived photograph that appears in many catalogs is a scene where friends of Modigliani are moving 
his possessions. One of the friends in the photo is Dr. Pau Alexandre. In the photo is a pillow very similar to the one painted 
in this painting including the fringe tassels and two of Modigliani’s paintings strewn on the ground. 
The skin tones of this painting are of a luminous quality likened by Modigliani and show transitions into rose tones at 
different parts of the body. The hair is a thick dark black with a bob in the back as shown in an attached illustration and as 
reported as part of Kiki’s hair style before being changed later to the Mikado style by Man Ray. The shoulders and arms are 
styled as those of Kiki’s and the legs are positioned similar to several leg positions in the attached drawings by Modigliani.
