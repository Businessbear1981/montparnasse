# Camille Aubry — character bible

*Locked 30 May 2026. The one fictional character in a cast of real people. Composite. Disclosed in the Author's Note on Sources. Built so the working relationship at the Restellini lab has somewhere honest to live in the writing without putting any real Restellini employee's words on a printed page.*

---

## One-line

The senior pigment chromatographer at Institut Restellini Analyses Scientifiques, Geneva Freeport, who runs the X-ray microfluorescence and forensic chemistry on the small Modigliani canvas at the centre of the book — and who, across four years, becomes Richard Triberg's working partner and the book's final voice.

## What she does at the lab

She runs the **scientific** half of every Restellini authentication: infrared reflectography to read underdrawings, digital X-ray radiography to read the substrate, X-ray microfluorescence spectroscopy to identify pigments without taking a sample, gas chromatography on the rare occasions a tiny scrape can be justified. She is the person who decides whether a 1917 painting's lead-white was made in Belgium or Germany or England by reading the trace metals — and from the trace metals, the decade and sometimes the year. She has signed off on twenty-three Restellini certifications in eight years. Twelve of them eventually entered the catalogue raisonné. Eleven of them did not.

She is forty years old. She is the most experienced pigment chromatographer in Switzerland under fifty. Her bench is in a basement laboratory directly under the vault that holds the Modigliani.

## Temperament — Richard's exact opposite

| Richard | Camille |
|---|---|
| Intuition, narrative, literary archaeology | Pure data, refuse to speculate past the chromatograph |
| Pushes ahead on a hunch | Pulls him back to what the numbers can support |
| Holden + Thompson on five hours of sleep | French calm, two espressos a day, leaves the lab at six |
| Drinks too much wine | Drinks two glasses of a single Saint-Joseph on a Friday |
| Will lose his shirt if Marc says no | Will lose her credibility if Marc says yes for the wrong reasons |
| Falls in love with the woman in the photograph | Falls in love with the molecule under the microscope |

She is not a romantic foil. She is a working partner. Their friction is the engine of every modern chapter. Their final agreement is the book's pivot.

## What she looks like

The book describes her sparingly. The reader knows: dark hair worn shoulder-length and tucked behind one ear, no jewellery in the lab beyond a thin steel chain with her keycard, a square jaw, an unsmiling resting face that Richard mistakes for impatience for the first two months until he realises it is simply concentration. She wears a white lab coat over plain dark clothes when she is at the bench and a charcoal-grey wool coat over plain dark clothes when she is not.

She speaks excellent English with a slight Lyonnais accent that comes through on words containing *r.* She speaks French with Richard only when she is furious with him, which has happened three times in four years.

---

## Why she invented

Three reasons, in order:

1. **The working-relationship dynamic at the lab needs somewhere to live.** Real people work at Restellini. Putting fictional dialogue and inner-life detail on any of them would be a problem. A disclosed composite solves it cleanly.
2. **The book's final voice needs to be a woman.** It cannot be Marc (he is the authority figure at the end of the corridor). It cannot be Alba Berti (she is the door-opener but stays loyal to Marc). The book's last sentence is a phone call between Richard and Camille — *Camille calls Richard,* not Marc emails him — and a male-only final voice would undercut the entire Kiki spine.
3. **She is the counter-current to Richard's intuition.** Without her, Richard's literary archaeology runs unchecked. The book needs the scientific brake on the literary engine, and the brake needs a person.

## Three rules she lives by

1. **The chromatograph is the only witness.** What the chromatograph says, she will defend in court. What it does not say, she will not invent.
2. **The lab does not love the painting.** Marc loves the painting. Richard loves the painting. Camille is paid to *not* love the painting. She trusts the painting more for it.
3. **Leave the lab at six.** Once, eighteen months into the dig, Richard called her at eleven at night from a hotel in Vienna with a question. She answered on the second ring. The next morning at the lab she handed him a note that read: *Richard. The lab is closed at six. The work waits. So do I.* He never called her after six again.

---

## What she will not do

- Speculate past her data.
- Sign anything on Marc's behalf.
- Discuss other clients' canvases with Richard.
- Drink with Richard during the work week. (Friday after seven is the negotiable line.)
- Use the word *muse* about Alice Prin. *Sitter,* always.

---

## What she will do

- Run a chromatograph on the unscheduled at her own initiative if she catches a hunch the bench data is missing something.
- Tell Marc to his face when she thinks he is wrong, in French, with the door closed.
- Drive Richard from Le Bourget to Geneva personally because she does not trust him not to fall asleep at the wheel after a Prague-Paris flight.
- Find the 2012 Wildenstein file that Marc had forgotten existed.
- See the X on the back of the canvas first.
- Make the closing call.

---

## A representative line of dialogue

*"Richard. The pigment says December 1919 at the earliest and January 1920 at the latest. The pigment does not know about your shared studio. The pigment does not know about your Nevinson. The pigment knows about the factory in Namur that closed in March of 1921 and the lead it was making in the eight months before it closed. Everything else you are going to have to bring yourself. But you can bring it now. The chromatograph cleared the way. Go."*

(He went.)

## Disclosure language for the published book

In the front-matter Author's Note on Sources:

> *Camille Aubry is a composite character, the one fictional figure in a cast of otherwise real people. She represents the kind of senior pigment chromatographer who would in fact run the X-ray microfluorescence and forensic chemistry on a painting at this stage of a Restellini authentication. No real Restellini employee is the basis for her, and no real Restellini employee speaks the dialogue attributed to her. She exists so the working relationship at the lab has somewhere honest to live in the book.*
