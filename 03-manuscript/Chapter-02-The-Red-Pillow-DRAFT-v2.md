# Chapter II — The Red Pillow

*Working draft v2, 29 May 2026. Revised against the v1 with three corrections: (1) Modi throughout, not Modigliani; (2) the 1917 scene strips all foreshadowing — no "she will be called Kiki," no "the painting will outlive everyone," no narrator-as-time-traveler; (3) Richard's voice in the present strips the "this was when my life changed" announcements. The end is not at the beginning. The reader catches up.*

---

If you really want to know about it, I found her the second time on a Tuesday afternoon in a museum gift shop, which I had not gone into a museum gift shop on purpose since I was twelve years old, and I am only telling you that now because the rest of what follows requires you to understand I was not looking. People who write books like this always say they were not looking. I really wasn't.

It was a postcard. The Tate had a stack of them on a wire rack by the door, the kind of rack that wobbles when you spin it. The postcard was of a painting called *An Artist Studio in Montparnasse,* by a British painter I had heard of exactly once — Christopher Richard Wynne Nevinson. C. R. W. The painting itself had hung in the Tate, the postcard said, since 1927. It had been donated by H. G. Wells, of all people, who I had not known collected paintings, but apparently he had, and apparently he had given this one away.

I bought the postcard for one pound forty. I did not know yet why.

What I knew was that there was a red pillow on the couch in the background of the painting, and the red pillow looked exactly like the red pillow on the couch in the painting Bill had spent the last three years of his life trying to authenticate. The red pillow with the greenish tassel trim, the one Modi had painted under his sitter in 1917. The one in the small canvas — forty centimetres by thirty-three — that was at that moment in a back room in Bratislava under a velvet cloth.

You could not see the painting on the Nevinson postcard. You could see the studio it had been painted in.

I went back to my hotel. I called Bill. I told him I thought I had just found Modi's 1917 studio in a Tate postcard for one pound forty.

Bill was quiet for a long time. Then he said, *come home.*

I caught the morning flight.

**CIRCA MONTPARNASSE, AUTUMN 1917**

The studio is on the rue de la Grande Chaumière, on the third floor, two tall windows facing north. The windows are open. There is no coal. The two painters who share the room have an arrangement: Nevinson in the morning, Modi in the afternoon. The afternoon has begun.

The red velvet pillow is on the couch by the wall. The pillow has a greenish tassel trim. The white shawl is over the back of the chair. The green hat is not on the chair yet.

Modi is at the easel with a brush in his right hand. He is thirty-one. He has been coughing for about a year now. He has bought the lead white on credit because he has bought everything on credit since the spring. The canvas in front of him is small — forty by thirty-three — and the ground is wet because he laid it down three days ago and is impatient. The signature is going to go in the lower right corner. He has not put it there yet.

Sisley Huddleston is sitting in the corner. Huddleston is forty. He writes about Paris for the *Christian Science Monitor* and writes literary letters for a London weekly that pays in pounds. He has a notebook open on his knee. He has not written anything yet. He is here because he is writing a piece on Montparnasse for an American magazine and Nevinson has agreed to be sketched while he paints, which is a small vanity of Nevinson's. Huddleston is watching the door.

There is a knock that is not quite a knock — more of a push. The door opens. A girl with rain in her hair steps in. She is laughing because she has run up the stairs. She is sixteen. She has a green hat in her hand.

Modi turns from the easel.

He does not say anything to her. He does not say anything to anyone in the room ever, in any of the accounts. He lifts his chin slightly — the way a man does when he wants someone to sit — and points at the couch with the red pillow on it.

She crosses the room.

She puts the green hat on the chair. She puts it next to the white shawl. She does not look at Nevinson, who has stopped painting at his own easel. She does not look at Huddleston in the corner, who is now writing something down. She unbuttons her coat and lays it over the arm of the couch. She takes off her shoes. She lies down on her side on the red pillow.

She folds her hands below her left knee.

Modi looks at her hands for a long time before he begins.

Huddleston, in the corner, writes one sentence in the notebook. Nine years and four months later he will pull that sentence out of his desk in his Paris apartment and copy it into a letter, by hand, in black ink, and post the letter to the Paris edition of the *New York Herald Tribune,* where it will be printed on the twenty-fourth of February, 1927, and reprinted in many English newspapers in the days that follow, and the sentence will be the sentence that opens the letter, and the sentence is *the girl on the couch.*

For now Huddleston only writes the sentence and underlines it.

Modi paints her hands.

He paints them first because he always paints the hands first. He paints them folded below her left knee. He paints them slowly. He paints them carefully. He paints them with a brush that he has been using for two years and that nobody has thought to replace.

Outside the window the rain is letting up.

In the room nobody speaks.

✱ ✱ ✱

I caught the morning flight. I had the Nevinson postcard in my breast pocket. I kept taking it out and looking at it. The red pillow was right there in the corner of the painting, on a couch, in a studio, in a building in Paris that I had walked past a hundred times in the past three years and never once looked up at.

Bill met me at the airport. He looked at the postcard for thirty seconds and put it in his pocket and said, *let's go to my office.*

We went to his office. He closed the door behind us. He pulled a folder out from the bottom drawer of his desk. The folder was thick. The folder had been in that drawer the entire three years I had known him.

He opened the folder and put it on the desk in front of me.

The first page was a Xeroxed clipping from a London newspaper. The clipping was dated the twenty-fourth of February, 1927. The headline of the letter was the name of the person who had written it.

*Sisley Huddleston.*

Bill watched me read it.

"I have been waiting four years to find someone who would see what you just saw," Bill said.

I did not say anything. I was reading the letter.

The letter was about a painting. The painting was at the Tate. The painting had been altered. The man who had altered the painting was named at the bottom of the letter. The man who had altered the painting was named C. R. W. Nevinson.

I read the letter twice.

I looked up at Bill.

"Where is the original sketch?" I asked.

Bill smiled. It was the first time I had seen Bill smile in three years.

"That," he said, "is the rest of your life."
