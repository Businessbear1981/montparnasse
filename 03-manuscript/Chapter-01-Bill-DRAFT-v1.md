# Chapter I — Bill

*Working draft v1, 29 May 2026. ~2,200 words. The chapter that introduces the cast — Richard, Bill, Modi, Kiki, the painting, and the hundred-and-ten-year question — so that Chapter II's fade lands on a reader who knows who is in the room. No foreshadowing. No end-at-the-beginning. The reader catches up.*

---

I should probably tell you, before any of this gets going, that I had not heard of Bill on the morning the phone rang. People who write books like this usually have some version of *we were destined* in the third or fourth paragraph. Bill and I were not destined. Bill and I were a wrong number.

The phone rang at four-twelve in the afternoon on a Wednesday. It was March, 2022. I was at home in the Napa Valley. I picked it up because I was waiting on a different call about a different thing that I will not bother explaining because it has nothing to do with anything that follows. A man's voice said, *is this Richard Triberg?* I said yes. The voice said, *I am sorry to bother you. I was told you might know something about Italian paintings from before 1920.*

I said, *I don't, really.*

The voice said, *that is not what I was told.*

There was a long pause. Long enough that I almost put the phone down. Then the voice said his name. *Bill,* he said. *Bill is what people call me.* He did not give his last name. He did not give it on that call and he did not give it the first time we met and he did not give it for nine and a half months, which is something I tell you now because it is going to matter later, and because by then I had stopped wanting to know.

He asked me if I had ever heard of a painter named Amedeo Modigliani.

I told him I had. Everybody has, even if they have not, because the long faces are on every dorm-room poster from California to Berlin and the dying-young-in-Paris part is in every art-history-for-non-majors textbook in the western hemisphere. Modi. Italian. Livorno, 1884. Paris, 1920. Tubercular meningitis at thirty-five, with a pregnant common-law wife on his floor who would jump from a window the next morning, and a body of work — a hundred and twenty paintings, plus drawings, plus the lost sculpture — that the world had taken about forty years to start pricing correctly. Everybody has heard of Modi.

Bill said, *good. Then I will not have to waste either of our afternoons explaining who he was.*

He asked me if I had ever heard of a woman named Alice Prin.

I told him I had not.

He said, *that is what I was afraid of. Alice Prin is what we are talking about.*

He told me he had a painting. He had had it for three years. He had been working on it for three years. He had not shown it to anyone except a Czech art historian named Otto Urban and a forensic chemist in Rome named Massimo who I would meet later and who would not survive the year. The painting was small. It was forty centimetres by thirty-three. It had been signed in the lower right corner in black paint with the word *modigliani.* He believed it was Modi's. He believed it had been painted in the autumn of 1917 in a studio on the rue de la Grande Chaumière. He believed the sitter was Alice Prin.

He believed all of this. He could not yet prove it.

That was where I came in.

I asked him why he was calling me. I had a credible enough excuse — I run a small fine-art brokerage out of a converted warehouse on the Richmond waterfront, I had been doing it ten years, I had handled enough Modigliani-adjacent material to be a working number in a fairly small Rolodex. But I had never authenticated anything. I had never wanted to. Authentication is for the experts and the experts know who they are and the experts will tell you, with great force, who is not one of them.

Bill said, *I am not asking you to authenticate it. I am asking you to help me sell it once the authentication clears.*

I said, *clears with who?*

He said, *Restellini.*

I had heard of Marc Restellini. Everybody who has spent more than three minutes in the Modigliani world has heard of Marc Restellini. He is the Frenchman who took over the cataloguing of Modigliani's work from a man named Christian Parisot, who Restellini's people will not let you mention in their presence, and he is the only man on earth whose signature on a piece of paper can take a small unidentified canvas out of a back room and put it into the catalogue raisonné and onto a public auction block at a price that will end three or four people's old lives and begin three or four people's new ones.

I asked Bill, *what does Restellini say?*

Bill said, *I have not asked him yet.*

I asked Bill, *why have you not asked him yet?*

Bill said, *I have been waiting until I had the right person standing next to me. Will you drive up here on Saturday and look at it?*

There was a sentence on the back of my tongue that wanted to come out, the kind of sentence a working broker says to a stranger on the phone in the middle of an afternoon in March, which is some polite version of *let us send some images first and see if there is anything to talk about.* The sentence did not come out. I do not know why. I have tried since to remember whether there was something in Bill's voice that made it not come out, and I cannot remember that there was. What I remember is that the sentence was on the back of my tongue, and then it was not, and what came out instead was, *yes. I will drive up on Saturday.*

I drove up that Saturday.

The address Bill had given me on the phone was an old building. The stairs were carpeted in dark green. There was a smell that I want to say was old paper but that was probably just the old building. At the top of the stairs was a single door with a brass plate that said WILLIAM and nothing else.

I had been expecting Bill to be sixty. Bill was eighty-one.

He opened the door himself. He was a small man, thin, white hair, an old grey cardigan over a yellow shirt. He shook my hand with both of his. He looked me in the eye for a long time. He said, *thank you for coming.*

The office was one room. There was a kitchen table in the middle with two chairs at it. There was a wall of bookshelves. There was a window that looked out over a small back lot. There was a small kettle on a side table. There was nothing on the walls. There were no paintings.

He sat me at the kitchen table. He made tea. He put it in front of me. He sat down across from me. He did not bring out the painting.

He talked for two hours and forty minutes.

He told me about Alice Prin. He started with where she was born. Châtillon-sur-Seine, in the Côte d'Or, in the autumn of 1901. He told me about her grandmother, who raised her until she was twelve. He told me about her mother, who pulled her to Paris because the mother had a new boyfriend. He told me about the cafés on the Boulevard Edgar Quinet where Alice modelled at fourteen because she was hungry. He told me about Soutine giving her the name Kiki, or maybe Pascin giving her the name Kiki, depending on whose account you believed. He told me about a journalist named Henri Broca who crowned her *Queen of Montparnasse* in a cabaret in 1922, with a paper crown and a glass of cheap champagne, when she was twenty-one.

He told me about Modi.

He told me about Modi being twenty-eight when he met her, then thirty-one when she was finally old enough to sit. He told me about Modi sketching her at the Rotonde, at Chez Rosalie's, on the back of menus, on the inside of cigarette boxes, for the entire two years between when they met in 1915 and when the small canvas was painted in the autumn of 1917. He told me about Modi's lungs.

He told me about the painting.

He had not brought it out yet. He talked about it the way you talk about a person you have lived next to for a long time. He told me what it looked like. He told me the dimensions — forty by thirty-three — without having to check. He told me about the red pillow under the sitter. He told me about the white shawl in the corner. He told me about the signature in black paint in the lower right.

He told me where the painting had been for the hundred and four years between when it was painted and when he had found it. He told me about a Slovak family in Bratislava who had inherited it from a cousin who had taken it from an apartment in Paris in the autumn of 1942 under circumstances he did not entirely want to describe. He told me about the moment, ten years ago, when one of the great-grandchildren in Bratislava had brought it out of a closet to show a relative, and the relative had said, *do you understand what this is.* He told me about the appraisal that had been done in 2013, in Prague, by an expert whose name he would not say. The appraisal had been filed with the Municipal Court of Prague in connection with a probate matter. The appraisal said the painting was authentic. The appraisal said the painting was worth seventy million dollars.

He told me about the trip to the Wildenstein Institute in Paris in 2012, when the painting had been sent for examination, and Marc Restellini had been the curator of the Wildenstein, and the painting had been there for two months, and the Wildenstein had told the owners they thought the painting was authentic but they would need more documentation before they would write it into the catalogue raisonné. He told me about how the Wildenstein had closed, eventually, and Restellini had spun off his own institute, and the work had stalled for ten years while the documentation that the painting needed had not been produced because nobody had yet known what shape the documentation should take.

He told me he had spent the last three years producing it.

He had not yet told me what the documentation was. He paused, and he poured more tea, and he asked me whether I wanted to see the painting now.

I said yes.

He stood up. He walked over to the window. He took down the venetian blind and folded it and set it on the floor. He went over to a small closet by the bookshelf. He opened the closet. He took out a wooden crate the size of a hatbox. He brought the crate to the kitchen table. He set it down between us. He opened the lid. He lifted the painting out of the crate. He set the painting on the table between us, propped against a stack of three books.

She was sitting on a red pillow with a greenish tassel.

Her legs were crossed. Her hands were folded below her left knee.

She had black hair cut to the jaw. She had her eyes closed. Her mouth was slightly open. The room around her was dark — browns and ochres and greens — and a white tablecloth hung from a still life in the upper right corner, and a wooden cabinet was barely visible in the upper left, and a piece of reddish-brown drapery hung along the lower left edge. The signature was in the lower right in black paint and it said *modigliani.*

I looked at her for what Bill later told me was eleven minutes.

When I finally spoke, what I said was, *who is she.*

Bill said, *that is what we have to prove.*

We sat with the painting between us for another twenty minutes. Bill did not say anything else. When I left, he walked me down the green-carpeted stairs and shook my hand again with both of his and said, *call me when you have decided.*

I drove home.

I did not sleep that night.

By Monday morning I had decided.

I called Bill from my office at nine-fifteen. He picked up on the second ring. I said, *I am in. What do you need me to do.*

He said, *come back up here on Saturday. Bring an overnight bag. I have a hundred and ten years of documentation to walk you through, and we are going to need every minute of the next three years to put it in front of Marc Restellini.*

I went back up on Saturday with an overnight bag.

That is how it began.
