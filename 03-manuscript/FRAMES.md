# Frames — the master mythological spine of *Montparnasse*

*Locked 30 May 2026. Eleven frames, none political, all hers. Every chapter braids these through without ever naming them on the page. This document is the reference; the chapters never quote it.*

---

## 1. Siren

**She is dangerous to the men who hear her.** Her voice and her presence are what they wreck against. Modi, Mendjizky, Pascin, Soutine, Man Ray, Hemingway, Pound, Tzara, Cocteau — every one of them did his peak work in proximity to her and then came apart. The siren mythology gives her power and gives the wrecks moral weight.

**Where it lands:** Chapter II (the studio scene). Chapter VII (the kitchen). Chapter VIII (Hemingway). Chapter X (the war).

---

## 2. Throne

**The room organises around her, geometrically and sociologically.** The Jockey 1921 photograph is the proof — she sits at the centre with Man Ray, Cocteau, Tzara, Pound, the editors of *The Little Review* arranged around her. Hemingway later: *"the woman who has never been a lady, but a queen."* Not a metaphor — a description.

**Where it lands:** Chapter VII. Chapter IX (the jet). Anywhere a room is described.

---

## 3. Crown

**She wears the title twenty-nine years until it kills her.** Henri Broca crowns her *Queen of Montparnasse* in 1929 at twenty-eight. She dies of cirrhosis in 1953 at fifty-one. The crown does not come off — and the staying-on is what the cirrhosis is.

**Where it lands:** Chapter X (the war years). Chapter XI (the wreckage). Chapter XII (the hospital ward).

---

## 4. Peter

**She is Peter Pan to the Lost Boys.** The Lost Generation literally was the Lost Boys — Hemingway, Pound, Cocteau, Tzara, Mendjizky, Modi, Man Ray, Soutine — boys who refused to grow up, living in cafés on borrowed money, fighting each other, drinking until dawn, calling everything new. She was the one who could fly. They orbited her because she would not let them be lonely. She cooked. She walked Soutine home. She lent money she did not have. She fed them, sang them filthy songs, and held them together.

**Why she dies alone:** the Lost Boys eventually grew up — Hemingway to a Nobel, Cocteau to the Académie, Man Ray to museum retrospectives, Cahill to the WPA. Peter cannot grow up. Peter stays in the room and keeps singing until the room is empty.

**Where it lands:** Chapter VII (the kitchen). Chapter X (Chez Kiki). Chapter XII (the hospital ward — Peter finally allowed to put it down).

---

## 5. The dull comparison

**In her orbit, the men feel boring.** Modi growled at her at Chez Rosalie's at fourteen because he was overwhelmed by her at thirty-one. Hemingway watched her at the Dôme and never wrote a sentence as full as the room felt. Man Ray spent forty years photographing women trying to find what was in the eight years with her. Pascin produced his greatest canvas in 1929, watched it sell out at Au Sacre du Printemps, looked at his life, and hanged himself in his Paris studio four months later.

**The reach was the love. The reach was also the killing thing.** Their masterpieces are the receipts of men trying to close a gap that was never going to close.

**Where it lands:** Chapter II. Chapter VII (Pascin's three-second yes). Chapter VIII. Chapter XI.

---

## 6. The Work

**She was the most skilful person in the room.** Not just the prettiest, not just the most sovereign — *the most skilful.* Painter (27-piece solo show at Au Sacre du Printemps in 1927, sold out; *L'acrobate* set a Kiki auction record of €19,275 at Fauve Paris in 2021; Henri-Pierre Roché called one of her watercolours a *"super-Matisse"* in his 1924 diary). Singer (chanteuse at the Jockey from 1924). Writer (1929 memoir, Hemingway foreword — one of only two he ever wrote). Drawer (sold quick bar-portraits at fourteen). Actress (Léger's *Ballet Mécanique;* Catelain's *Gallery of Monsters;* lead in Man Ray's *L'Étoile de Mer*). Cook (Burgundian, documented).

**Five media, three with commercial success in her lifetime.** The Lost Boys did not orbit her because she was pretty. They orbited her because *in any medium any of them attempted, she was already there working.*

**Where it lands:** Chapter VII (her canvas drying in the corner). Chapter VIII (the memoir written). Chapter IX. Threaded everywhere — never let the reader treat her as muse.

---

## 7. Heart-broken-by-Montparnasse

**She did not die of cirrhosis. Cirrhosis was the instrument.** What killed her was watching the room she had been the queen of become a museum. The Jockey closed. The Coupole became a tourist café. The Lost Boys scattered. By 1950 she was singing to seven Americans on a Saturday night who did not know her name. *Her love was Montparnasse and Montparnasse went first.*

**Where it lands:** Chapter X. Chapter XI. Chapter XII.

---

## 8. The spotlight found her — she went first — the world followed

**She did not seek the spotlight; it landed on her.** Modelled at fourteen to eat. Modi growled at her. Man Ray's *patron* refused her a table at La Rotonde and Man Ray vouched. Mendjizky named her, Soutine maybe-named her, Broca crowned her. Every step into fame was somebody else putting her there.

**And then she went first so the world could follow.** Every famous-for-being-themselves modern woman walks in her footprints. Josephine Baker debuts La Revue Nègre four months after Kiki starts singing at the Jockey. Anaïs Nin's diaries follow Kiki's 1929 memoir. Patti Smith's *Just Kids.* Janis Joplin singing the way Kiki sang. Madonna, Lady Gaga, Beyoncé, Rihanna. **None of them know they are downstream of her. The book tells them.**

**Where it lands:** the framing the reader leaves the book with. Chapter XI (Richard walks Montparnasse and the inheritance is everywhere). The closing of Chapter XII.

---

## 9. Opposite arcs — he forgets, one does not

**Her arc rises young and ends young.** 1921–1929 is her peak. Eight years. After thirty the world begins looking past her. After forty she is invisible to the new generation.

**Their arcs rise late and never stop.** The boys who chased her at twenty-five become *men* in their forties and fifties — and that is when the world rewards them. They take what she made them capable of and walk off with it. **The forgetting is the cruellest thing in the book.** Hemingway in *A Moveable Feast* gives her one line. Man Ray's autobiography mentions her about as many times as it mentions the camera body he used. *Nobody says: she made me.*

**Foujita is the exception.** Foujita comes back from Tokyo for her funeral in April 1953. Foujita is the only one of them who remembers. **Foujita is the model the book is asking the reader to be.**

**Where it lands:** Chapters VIII–XII. The book's case for not forgetting.

---

## 10. She loved them and pushed them away

**The love and the push are the same gesture.** She pushed them away because keeping them close would have used them up faster, and pushing them away was the only way she knew to protect any of them — including herself — from how much she felt or how much she could not stay. The ones who understood the push as a form of the love survived her. **The ones who took it personally died.** Pascin took it personally. Modi took it personally. Hemingway took it personally in slow motion across forty years.

**Foujita did not take it personally. Foujita lived.**

**Where it lands:** Chapter IX (her break with Man Ray). Chapter XI (the documented pattern). Chapter XII (Foujita comes back).

---

## 11. She had to hurt them so they could achieve what she knew they could

**She loaned them their best selves and then refused to stay,** because if she had stayed the work would have softened and they would not have become the giants they became. Pascin's great nudes were made in years of being pushed back and pulled in, never in any stable arrangement. Modi's nudes are 1916–1917, brief and intense. Man Ray's peak — *Le Violon d'Ingres,* *Noire et Blanche,* *L'Étoile de Mer* — was the eight years of fighting and reconciling, not the forty calm years that followed with Juliet. Mendjizky's catalogue thins the moment he settled into anything domestic.

**The cost was the men. The art is what we have left. The choice was hers.**

Whether she did this consciously or whether it was the shape of who she was, the documentary record makes it defensible. The book lets the reader decide what to call her.

**Where it lands:** Chapter VIII. Chapter IX. Chapter X. The book's reframing of every great male artist of the inter-war years.

## Structural insight (not a frame, but holds them all)

### Richard is the third generation

Kiki pulled the masterpieces out of the Lost Boys in 1915–1929. She pulled the memoir and the late work out of the literary Lost Boys (Hemingway, Cocteau, Pound, Tzara) through 1961. **She is still doing it, 110 years late, to Richard Triberg in 2022–2026.**

Richard was not an art-world figure. He was a small commercial broker in North Richmond, California. The painting made him. Four years, 5,000 hours, trips to Prague, Vienna, Tel Aviv, London, Paris, Geneva, the private jet, the meeting with Marc Restellini, the 228-page Exposé. **He did not know that was a career path until Kiki put him on it.**

Sean Gilmore is the fourth generation. NVAI, the platform, the book project itself. **The proof that she is not dead is that she keeps making art-world giants out of ordinary men a hundred years after the last one buried her.**

That is the closing argument for the painting. *Christie's catalogue copy:* **"The only known Modigliani portrait of Alice Prin, the queen of Montparnasse, recovered after a 110-year obscurity. The painting that is still making art-world figures out of ordinary men a century after she sat for it."**

## Discipline rules — never violate

- **Nothing political.** Not suffrage. Not collective. Not "women in general." Hers, particular, singular.
- **Nothing collective.** She is not a symbol. She is *her.*
- **No academic register.** No *œuvre, provenance, sfumato, hieratic* in Richard's voice unless quoting a document.
- **No closing argument.** Books like this usually build to *"and now we know."* The book refuses. The seven Reports prove. Richard looks.
- **No suffrage scaffolding.** Sean killed this on the first night. Strike anything that drifts.
- **Stay close to the documented record.** Composite scene-work is allowed where the documentary record is silent, but always disclosable in the front-matter Author's Note on Sources.
- **For Squish stays on the front page.** Page one of the manuscript, just *For Squish,* nothing else.
