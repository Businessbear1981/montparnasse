# Tsuguharu Foujita — character bible

*Locked 30 May 2026. The loyal one. Real person. Lifelong friend of Kiki across thirty years, the only mourner at her burial, and the moral counter-evidence to the antagonist of this book.*

*Voice discipline note: when Foujita is in a scene, the prose register tightens toward the Bashō line — clean, image-precise, few words, much white space. Foujita painted on ivory grounds with a single fine black contour. The prose around him should look the same on the page.*

---

## One-line

The Japanese painter from Tokyo who arrived in Paris the year Kiki arrived from Châtillon, painted her into his first major success, shared the money with her, wrote a foreword to her memoir, was the only person at her burial in 1953, and walked away from Montparnasse forever rather than have it without her.

## What the Exposé documents (cited)

### The first sitting — the Camembert scene (p.33)

This is the founding anecdote of their friendship, and one of the most vivid Kiki scenes the source gives us. The first day she arrived to pose for Foujita:

> *She was naked beneath her coat, only pinning a small handkerchief at the neckline to indicate a dress. However, she didn't strip off but turned the tables by sitting behind the easel, and sternly told the artist not to move. By the time she had completed the portrait she had sucked and bitten all of the artist's pencils, lost his eraser and trodden a packet of Camembert into the carpet. She got up, demanded (and received) her fee and went out with the drawing under her arm, promptly selling it to a rich American in the café next door. The next day she returned and allowed him to paint a portrait of her.*

What this scene tells us: from minute one, Foujita let her flip the dynamic. He sat. He paid. He did not chase. *That is the temperament of the friendship for thirty years.*

### The 1922 success — the proceeds shared (p.35)

> *In 1922, Foujita had his first big success when he sold a painting of Kiki at an exhibition at Le Grand Palais. Titled Nu couché à la toile de Jouy, the painting sold for 8,000 francs. Foujita shared the proceeds with Kiki — a nearly unheard of gesture at that time.*

The Exposé treats this as the singular gesture. In an era when models received almost nothing and artists kept everything, Foujita split the money on his first major sale. He did not have to. The painting was his. The work was his. He shared because it was hers too.

*This is the act that defines him morally inside the book.*

### The 1929 memoir foreword

Foujita and Hemingway were the two writers Kiki chose for her memoir introductions (p.12). Hemingway's English foreword has been the famous one ever since. Foujita's French introduction is the equal companion in the original Henri Broca edition and gets shorter shrift in the Anglophone reception — *which the book can correct.*

### The romantic rumour, dismissed

> *It was also rumored that Foujita was her lover at the beginning of the relationship with Man Ray, though Foujita always insisted it was not true, of course we believe him.* (p.33)

The Exposé's wink ("of course we believe him") suggests Richard's own read: they probably slept together at some point and it was incidental to a much deeper non-romantic bond. The book should follow Richard's lead — friendship is the load-bearing wall, sex is footnoted.

### The pubic-hair anomaly scene (animated film, p.55)

The 2013 French animated *Mademoiselle Kiki et les Montparnos* contains a scene around 5:23–5:57 where Foujita first encounters Kiki's pubic-hair anomaly (she had a condition that prevented its growth, p.55) and handles it as a light moment between friends. The Exposé treats this scene as accurate to the historical relationship — *Foujita knew the most intimate thing about her body and treated it with affection, not exposure.* Other photographers did not respect her request to retouch the negatives. Foujita always did. **He was a man who could be trusted with what mattered.**

### The burial — April 30, 1953 (p.38)

> *Kiki's life-long friend, Tsuguharu Foujita, is said to have been the only person present at her burial. The Japanese master vowed that he would never again return to Montparnasse. It wouldn't be the same without Kiki.*

This is the closing image of the historical arc of the book. The Exposé gives us almost nothing more — no description of the cemetery, no description of his face, no description of the weather, no other mourners present (Mark Braude's *Kiki Man Ray* and Edith de Belleville's archive may have more). What the source DOES give us is enough: *one man, at the grave of one woman, on one spring morning in 1953, and his decision afterwards.*

## Broader biography (external — to verify before press)

- One of the most celebrated Japanese painters of the 20th century. Brought a Japanese line sensibility to Parisian modernism — the famous **ivory-white ground with a fine black contour**, faces and bodies rendered like wood-block prints translated into oil.
- Member of the École de Paris alongside Modigliani, Soutine, Chagall, Kisling.
- Returned to Japan during WWII; produced large-scale war paintings for the Imperial government — *controversial and beyond the scope of this book. Out of frame.*
- Returned to France after the war, converted to Catholicism in 1959 (taking the name Léonard), died in Zürich 1968, buried at the Notre-Dame de la Paix chapel he had decorated himself in Reims.

*The book stays inside the Paris years and the Kiki relationship. We do not adjudicate his wartime record. That's somebody else's book.*

## Role in the book's three Acts

### Act 1 — RISE (1913–1922)

- **Arrives in Paris 1913** — the year Kiki arrives from Châtillon. *Two strangers walk into the same neighbourhood from opposite ends of the world.*
- **The first sitting** — the Camembert scene. The book gets a full scene here. *This is also a great example of Kiki's early character — she did not arrive as a passive muse, she arrived flipping the easel around.*
- **The friendship forms.** They become each other's regular company at the Rotonde, the Dôme, the cheap meals after the long mornings.

### Act 2 — PEAK (1922–1929)

- **1922 — the Salon d'Automne success.** Foujita's *Nu couché à la toile de Jouy.* He shares the money with her. The book gets a scene of this — perhaps the conversation at his studio when he hands her her half. *Spare. Bashō register. He says little. The handing is the line.*
- **Throughout the 1920s** — the cafés. He is the friend who is *always there.* Man Ray comes and goes. Broca comes later. Foujita is at the table the whole decade.
- **1929 — the foreword.** When she asks two writers to introduce her memoir, he is one of them. Hemingway gets the English; Foujita gets the French original. *Both are friends. Only one stays.*

### Act 3 — FALL (1930–1953)

- **The post-Man Ray years** — Foujita is the one who keeps showing up. Broca falls away. The cabaret closes. The drugs take her down. *Foujita is still at the table.*
- **The war years** — he is back in Japan; she is in Paris under occupation, struggling, sometimes hospitalised. The book can acknowledge the absence honestly — *he was not there in those years; nobody was.* When he returns to France after the war, the city is changed and so is she.
- **The 1953 hospital scene** (p.12 mentions "Painters Florian and Foujita at The Levee of The Body of Kiki de Montparnasse at Hospital Laennec") — there is at least one moment before the burial when Foujita and another painter, Florian, are present at the body. The book can write that scene.
- **The burial — April 30, 1953.** The closing image of the historical arc. *One man at the grave. A Paris morning. The decision afterwards.*
- **The walk away.** He never returns to Montparnasse.

## Voice register when Foujita is in the scene

Foujita's painting style: ivory ground, single fine black contour, restraint, restraint, restraint. Japanese line meeting French oil.

The prose should match. **When Foujita is in a scene, the register tightens to Bashō.** Short sentences. Concrete nouns. Image-precise. White space on the page. No metaphor he would not have used himself.

**Examples of the register:**

*The pencils were sharp. He had sharpened them in the morning. By noon they were in her mouth, one at a time, the wood between her teeth, the lead going dull against her tongue. He did not say a thing. He watched her draw him. The carpet smelled of Camembert by three.*

*He handed her the envelope. Inside the envelope was four thousand francs. Four thousand francs was half of eight thousand. She looked at the envelope. She looked at him. He looked away.*

*The cemetery in April. Wet stone. A small group of men in dark coats walked past on the gravel toward another grave. He was alone. He was the only one. He looked at the wooden marker. He thought about returning to Tokyo. He decided he would not return to Montparnasse.*

That's the register. Restraint as devotion.

## What this character does that no one else does

He is the **counter-evidence** to the book's antagonist.

The antagonist we locked: *the same act that made them giants unmade her. The world keeps the work and forgets the giver.*

Foujita is the one man who **did not take the bargain.** He kept the work AND kept the giver. He shared the money. He came to the grave. He gave up Montparnasse rather than have it without her.

He proves the bargain was a choice. The other men chose one way. He chose the other. *The book stands on his single decision.*

## A representative line from him in the book

That's the friendship in nine words. The friendship that lasted thirty years.
