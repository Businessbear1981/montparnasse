# Chapter V — Bratislava

*Working draft v1, 30 May 2026. ~3,600 words. The week after Bill's funeral and the parcel. Richard flies to Bratislava on a Tuesday from SFO via Frankfurt. Meets Judr. Robert and Alexander in an apartment on Heydukova ulica. Sees the painting in the back room. The first sight. The painting passes into his possession in the legal sense — not as owner, but as broker under the freshly-signed agreement that pre-dates anything Sean and Richard later formalised. Threaded with: Circa Montparnasse 1920–1921, the wilderness year after Modi dies, Alice Prin at eighteen alone in the rue de la Grande Chaumière studio, Mendjizky finding her, the naming.*

---

I flew to Bratislava on the Tuesday after Bill's funeral. I bought the ticket on the Monday afternoon at a public-library Travelocity terminal — I was not staying in my own house with the parcel any more, I was using the library's computers because something about being in the library made me feel less alone — and I bought a coach ticket out of San Francisco to Frankfurt to Bratislava because the next direct on Lufthansa was Thursday and I could not wait until Thursday.

The flight from SFO was nine and a half hours. I did not sleep. I had brought the parcel with me in a carry-on bag — *all* of the parcel, the letter, the zibaldone, the three Xerox folders, the two photographs, the manila envelope marked *The Names,* the business card, the small white envelope I had already opened, and the line of pencil on the thin archival paper. I had not let it out of my hand at the security check at SFO. The TSA officer had asked me what was in the bag and I had told him *family papers,* which was the first lie I had ever told a TSA officer and which was also, as it turned out, true.

I read the zibaldone on the plane.

Three hundred and forty pages of Bill's handwriting across thirty-nine years. Each page dated. The first dated the second of October, 1986. The last dated the eleventh of October, 2025, which was thirty-three days before he died. The handwriting changed across the decades the way a man's handwriting does — looser, slower, shaky in the last hundred pages — but the discipline of one line per observation, one date per line, did not change.

The zibaldone was the search.

Page one: *Aaron's painting must still exist. Margaret's father confirmed the family carried it out of Paris in the spring of 1923 and lost it again in the autumn of 1942. The trail goes cold in Vichy. I am going to look.*

Page seventeen: *Three days at the Library of Congress. Nothing.*

Page sixty-four: *I have been wrong about Lithuania.*

Page one hundred and three: *Wildenstein examined a small Modigliani nude in 2012. The dimensions match. I am going to write Marc Restellini.*

Page one hundred and fifty: *Marc did not reply.*

Page two hundred and eight: *Marc replied. The painting is in Slovakia. The owner is a man named Judr. Robert. He does not want to sell.*

Page two hundred and forty-one: *I am eighty years old. I am running out of time. I need an apprentice.*

Page two hundred and forty-five: *A young broker named Richard Triberg came up in my search this morning. He runs a small art operation four blocks from my house. I have walked past his office to the post office for seven years and never once looked up.*

The hand on page two hundred and forty-five was the same hand that had written page one. The man on page two hundred and forty-five was not the same man.

I closed the zibaldone somewhere over Greenland and I sat with it in my lap for the rest of the flight and I did not open it again. I did not want to read what he had written about me. I read the last two pages eventually — three years and seven months later, on the day Marc Restellini signed the certificate — and I am going to tell you what they said when we get to that chapter. Not before.

The flight landed in Frankfurt at six in the morning local time. The connection to Bratislava was at eleven. I drank four espressos in a café in Terminal 1 and I called the Slovak phone number again from a payphone because I did not trust the SIM in my American iPhone to connect cleanly.

The man who answered, the same man who had answered three nights earlier in Richmond, said, *Mr. Triberg. You are in Frankfurt?*

I said, *yes.*

He said, *we will pick you up at Bratislava M. R. Štefánik Airport. A blue Škoda. Look for the man with a grey beard reading *Pravda.* That is Alexander. I will be in the back seat. My name is Robert.*

I said, *thank you.*

He said, *please. Eat something. You will not eat well at our house.*

I ate a *brötchen* with cheese at the café. I drank a fifth espresso. I boarded the connection at ten-forty.

Bratislava in late November is a colour I had not seen before. The sky was the colour of old graphite. The Danube was darker than the sky. The Škoda was, in fact, blue.

Alexander did have a grey beard.

The drive to Heydukova ulica was twelve minutes. Robert sat in the back seat beside me. He was a short heavy man in his early sixties with a face that had been the face of a Bratislava lawyer for forty years before it had been the face of a man waiting in an apartment for a small painting to find its way to him. He shook my hand without smiling. He said, *Bill was a good man. I am sorry.*

I said, *thank you.*

He said, *the painting has been waiting.*

I said, *I know.*

The apartment was on the third floor of a Habsburg-era building two streets behind the National Theatre. The stairwell smelt of cabbage and tobacco. The apartment door was reinforced steel with three locks. Robert opened all three. We went in.

The flat was three small rooms and a kitchen. The front room was set up like a small office with two desks against the windows and a printer between them. The middle room was a sitting room with a worn Persian carpet and a low samovar on a side table that had been used recently. The back room was where the painting was.

Alexander did not come into the back room with us. Alexander sat down in the sitting room and lit a cigarette.

Robert opened the back-room door. He turned on a single brass lamp on a desk by the window. He went to a wooden wardrobe against the far wall and he opened it. Inside the wardrobe, on the bottom shelf, was a small wooden crate the size of a hatbox. The crate was wrapped in two old wool blankets that had once been blue and that had become, across decades, the colour of an old sea.

He lifted the crate out of the wardrobe.

He carried it to the desk under the lamp.

He set it down.

He removed the blankets one at a time. Each blanket he folded slowly and set aside on the corner of the desk. The crate underneath was made of unfinished pine and was held shut by four small brass clasps that had gone green with age.

He opened the four clasps.

He lifted the lid.

Inside the crate, on a bed of acid-free tissue paper that had been replaced — I would learn later — twice a year for sixteen years, was a small canvas.

Forty centimetres by thirty-three centimetres.

Oil on canvas.

A girl seated on a red velvet pillow with a greenish tassel.

Her legs were crossed.

Her hands were folded below her left knee.

She had black hair cut to the jaw.

Her eyes were closed.

Her mouth was slightly open.

The signature in the lower right read, in black paint, *modigliani.*

I stood for what Robert later told me was four minutes without saying anything.

Then I said, *Robert.*

He said, *yes.*

I said, *the photograph in Bill's parcel is the same painting.*

He said, *yes.*

I said, *the photograph was taken in 1917.*

He said, *we believe so.*

I said, *Bill's great-uncle Aaron stood in this room.*

Robert was quiet for a long moment.

Then he said, *Mr. Triberg. Aaron stood in the room this painting was painted in. Aaron did not stand in this room. This room is a Bratislava apartment that has had this painting in it since 1955. Aaron was dead by then. But you are not wrong about the connection. Aaron is in Bill's notebook on page eighty-seven and Aaron is in our family's papers on page three of the inheritance file in the kitchen, and the two pages are about the same morning in October of 1917, and the painting on the desk in front of you is the morning they are about.*

I sat down on the chair beside the desk.

I asked Robert if I could see the inheritance file.

He said, *of course. But first you will have a coffee with Alexander. And we will eat the bread Alexander's wife sent over. And then we will all read the papers together. The painting is patient. We are patient. You have travelled. Come, *prosím.* The sitting room.*

We went to the sitting room.

Alexander had made coffee. There was bread on the table. The bread had been baked that morning by Alexander's wife in Petržalka and it was a *kalács,* dark, sweet, dense. We ate the bread. We drank the coffee. We did not speak for a long time. The samovar steamed on the side table.

After a while Robert went to the kitchen and brought out the inheritance file. It was a green plastic three-ring binder. It was thick.

We read it together until evening.

**CIRCA MONTPARNASSE, OCTOBER 1920**

The studio on the rue de la Grande Chaumière is empty in the afternoons now. Modi has been dead nine months. Jeanne has been dead nine months and one day. The landlord has not yet rented the room because the landlord has not yet figured out what to do with the canvases stacked against the wall, the empty bottles in the corner, the half-mixed paint on the small table, the unfinished portrait of Jeanne on the easel that the family has not yet come to collect.

Alice Prin comes by sometimes.

She is nineteen. She is not Kiki yet. Mendjizky will not give her that name for another fifteen months. She is still Alice and she is alone and the small room in the rue Joseph-Bara she has been sleeping in does not feel like anywhere she is supposed to be, and so on Tuesdays and Thursdays she walks the four blocks to the rue de la Grande Chaumière in the late afternoon and she sits on the stone step outside the door of Modi's studio and she does not go in.

She does not go in because she is not allowed in. The landlord has the only key. Modi never gave her a key because Modi was never the giving-keys sort of man.

She sits on the step.

She does not stay long. She stays maybe ten minutes, maybe twenty. The light comes down the rue de la Grande Chaumière in the late afternoon at an angle that lays the shadow of the chimney pots across the cobbles in a particular way, and she likes the way the shadow moves, and she sits on the step until the shadow has crossed the gutter on the other side of the street, and then she gets up and she walks back to the rue Joseph-Bara.

She has had no money for three weeks.

She has been sleeping in the room of a girl named Thérèse Treize de Caro, who lets her, because Thérèse remembers being Alice's age and being broke. Thérèse goes out at night. Alice does not. Alice goes to the cafés in the afternoon when they are nearly empty and she draws portraits of the men who will pay her a franc each. She is paid in small change. She eats one meal a day at Chez Rosalie's — Rosalie still gives her bread, two years after Alice stopped working there.

She is being painted, in October 1920, by a Polish artist she has met twice. The Polish artist's name is Maurice Mendjizky. He is twenty-eight. He has paid her for two sittings — a franc each — and he has told her on the second sitting that he is going to paint her again, and again, and again, and that he is going to call her something other than Alice, but that he has not yet thought of what.

She has not yet told Mendjizky that she thinks the something other than Alice could be the small word the Bratislava-side cousins of Mendjizky's mother use for *little one* in their Yiddish, which she had overheard him use in his studio on the second sitting when he had stood up to fetch the tea and had muttered to himself, on the way to the kettle, *malenkaya kiki, malenkaya kiki, malenkaya kiki.*

She has not told him yet because she does not yet trust him.

She is going to trust him by Christmas.

By the Christmas of 1922 she will trust him enough that he will paint her four more times and she will let him give her the name in the public way, in front of the room at the Café Certa, on the night of New Year's Eve 1922, with one hand on her shoulder and a glass of cheap champagne in the other, and she will laugh and she will say *Kiki, alors, va pour Kiki,* and the name will stay.

But that has not happened yet.

In October 1920 she is still Alice. She is alone. She is broke. She is sitting on the stone step outside Modi's empty studio in the late afternoon watching the shadow of the chimney pots cross the gutter.

She is thinking about the apple she gave the tubercular twenty-eight-year-old colour merchant's apprentice three years and one week ago at the corner of the rue du Montparnasse and the rue Vavin. She does not remember his name. She does remember his face. She thinks about him sometimes.

She does not know that he wrote her a sentence in 1968 about that apple. She is never going to know.

She does not know that the sentence will be in a parcel in Bratislava one hundred and five years from now. She is never going to know that either.

She gets up off the step.

She walks back to the rue Joseph-Bara.

The light goes.

Robert and Alexander and I read the inheritance file until ten in the evening. We did not eat dinner. Alexander's wife came over at eight with a pot of soup and she set it on the table and she did not stay. Robert ladled the soup. We ate it standing up over the file.

The inheritance file proved everything Bill had written.

The painting had been in the Robert-family-side of the Bratislava genealogy since the autumn of 1955, when a man named Vlado — Robert's grandfather's first cousin once removed — had been given it by a man in Vienna who owed him a debt that the debt-side of the relationship had felt could only be settled with an object the giving-side had no business owning. Vlado had not known what he was being given. Vlado had thought it was a Czech amateur nude from the 1930s. Vlado had hung it in his back room behind a curtain because his wife had not approved of nudes in the front of the house. Vlado had died in 1971. The painting had passed to his daughter. The daughter had not approved of nudes in the front of the house either. The painting had stayed behind the curtain in the back room for forty-three more years before Robert's branch of the family had inherited it on the death of the daughter in 2013, and had taken it out from behind the curtain, and had looked at the signature in the lower right in black paint, and had said the four-letter word that is the same in Slovak as it is in English.

The man in Vienna had been a *Wehrmacht-Vermögensverwerter* in 1942.

He had liberated the painting from a Paris apartment on the seventeenth of August 1942.

The Paris apartment had belonged to a man whose name was on page three of the inheritance file, which was the page Robert and Alexander had been preparing to show me when I had asked to see the file.

The name on page three of the inheritance file was the name of Bill's grandfather.

Bill's grandfather had been Aaron's older brother.

Aaron had given Bill's grandfather the painting in the spring of 1923, three years after Aaron had begun coughing himself to death and four years after Alice Prin had given Aaron the apple at the corner of the rue du Montparnasse and the rue Vavin. Aaron had asked Bill's grandfather to keep the painting safe and to give it to one of Aaron's children when Aaron had children, which Aaron never had, because Aaron had died in October 1924 of tuberculosis on the same ward of the same hospital where Modi had died in January 1920.

Bill's grandfather had kept the painting in the small Paris apartment until the seventeenth of August 1942.

After that the family had not known where it was.

For seventy-one years.

Until Robert had taken the painting out from behind the curtain in his cousin Vlado's back room in Bratislava in the autumn of 2013 and had said the four-letter word.

I read the page three times.

The third time I read it I started crying. I had not been planning to. I am not the sort of man who cries at documents.

Alexander, who had been standing the whole evening, sat down in the chair across the table from me. He did not say anything. He poured me a small glass of *slivovica.* I drank the slivovica. I drank a second one. I did not stop crying. Robert did not stop me crying. Robert stood in the doorway of the kitchen looking at the painting on the desk in the back room across the apartment through the open door, and he was crying too, with his face turned away.

We sat in the kitchen until past one in the morning.

I caught the flight back to Frankfurt the next morning.

I carried the painting with me.

The paperwork that allowed me to carry the painting with me had been waiting in Robert's desk for nine years. The paperwork named me by name. The paperwork had been signed by Robert and Alexander and a notary in Bratislava on the twenty-third of August 2016, the morning after a long-distance phone call from a man named William in California, who had told them, *you will not have heard of him yet but his name is going to be Richard Triberg.*

I carried the painting through Bratislava M. R. Štefánik, through Frankfurt, through San Francisco, in a small wooden crate the size of a hatbox, in the overhead bin of a Lufthansa Airbus, in the passenger seat of my Toyota all the way up the Valley.

I set the crate on the dining-room table beside the parcel.

I called Margaret.

I told her I had it.

She said, *Richard. I am glad.*

She hung up.

I sat at the dining-room table for the rest of the night with my hand on the side of the crate. I did not open it. I had opened it once in Bratislava and I had carried it across two oceans and one continent and I did not need to see it again that night. It was enough to know it was on the table.

It is hard for me to tell you how it was enough.

But it was.
