# Chapter IV — For Richard — Find Her

*Working draft v1, 30 May 2026. ~4,800 words. The book's emotional centrepiece. Bill dies. The somber funeral. The morning after, his widow hands Richard a brown-paper package wrapped in twine, with three words in Bill's handwriting on the top: For Richard — find her. Modi dies on the same page, 104 years apart. The two losses sit on facing pages. The mantle passes. The eight frames braid through but never get named.*

---

I should tell you how Bill died because the way Bill died is part of what changed me, and I have spent the past two and a half years trying to write a version of the next four pages that did not have me in it, and every version was worse than the last, and so finally here you are getting it the way it actually happened, which is the version with me in it.

He died on a Wednesday.

He died on a Wednesday because for fourteen weeks he had been telling me he was going to die on a Wednesday. I am not saying he was a mystic about it. I am saying that the morphine pump on the bedside table in the upstairs front bedroom of the brown brick building on Macdonald Avenue had been on a fixed dose for fourteen weeks, and Bill — who had spent four decades looking at oil paintings and understood the slope of decay better than most physicians — had told me on the second Tuesday in September that what he was looking at was probably ninety-six days, plus or minus four. He was off by one.

He was right about Wednesday.

I had been in his office every Saturday for the last three years and most Mondays for the last fourteen weeks, and on the second Monday in November I had driven up at nine in the morning and found him in the chair by the window — the one chair, not the bed, he had wanted to die in the chair — and he had said to me, *come closer Richard,* and I had pulled the wooden kitchen chair over so my knees almost touched his knees, and he had taken both of my hands in his hands the way he had on the first day in March of 2022, two hands around mine, the same as the handshake at the door three years and eight months earlier, and he had looked at me for what was probably forty-five seconds without saying anything.

Then he had said, *the painting is real.*

I had said, *I know.*

He had said, *she is real.*

I had said, *I know.*

He had said, *find her.*

I had said, *I will.*

He had closed his eyes. He had not opened them for what was probably another forty seconds. Then he had opened them and he had looked at me and he had smiled the way Bill smiled, which was almost not a smile but more like a small loosening of the corners of his mouth, and he had said the only thing he had ever said about himself in three years and eight months that was not about the painting, which was, *I am glad it was you, Richard.*

I had not been able to say anything to that.

I had sat with him for another two and a half hours holding his hands. He had not spoken again. He had breathed in shallower and shallower draughts the way the dying breathe. The breath had become a small rasping thing that did not belong to the room any more, and then around three in the afternoon it had stopped, and the room was quiet, and his hands had still been warm in mine for what was probably twenty minutes after that, and I had not let them go.

His widow had come in around four. Her name is Margaret. I had only met Margaret once before, when she had brought us tea in the office on a Saturday in May of 2024, and we had not spoken much then. She had looked at her husband in the chair by the window for a long time, and then she had looked at me, and she had said, *thank you for being here.*

I had not been able to say anything to that either.

The funeral was on the following Tuesday at the Episcopal church on the corner of Garrard and Macdonald. I do not know why it was Episcopal. Bill had never mentioned a church. The minister read a passage from the Book of Wisdom that I have not been able to find again in any Bible I have looked in since. There were sixty-seven people in the pews. I knew none of them. I sat in the back row on the aisle in a grey suit I had bought the afternoon before at a Macy's in El Cerrito because it had not occurred to me until the night before the funeral that I owned no funeral clothes, and Margaret had seen me come in and had walked the length of the centre aisle and had taken me by the elbow and had walked me up to the front row, and she had sat me beside her, and we had sat through the whole thing in silence with her hand on my forearm, and I had cried twice — once during the Wisdom reading and once when the organist played a piece I will not name here because I do not want to ruin it for you the way it has been ruined for me — and Margaret had not cried at all.

The reception was at the brown brick building.

I had not been back inside the office since the Wednesday. Bill's chair by the window was empty. The morphine pump had been removed. Someone — Margaret, presumably — had put a single white peony in a small glass vase on the kitchen table in the place where the painting had once sat between us.

I stood in the doorway of the office for a long time. Margaret found me there.

She said, *Richard, come with me.*

I followed her down the green-carpeted stairs and out the back door of the brown brick building into a small courtyard with a wooden bench against the back fence and a lemon tree in a terracotta pot. The lemon tree had two lemons on it. The morning was cold for November in Richmond and Margaret had on a black wool cardigan over her dress.

She sat down on the bench. She motioned for me to sit beside her. I sat.

She said, *William left you something.*

She had on her lap a small parcel about the size of a hatbox. It was wrapped in brown paper that had gone soft around the edges from being handled, and it was tied with a length of twine in a single bow that had been tied many times across the past months by hands that had known what they were tying it for. On the top of the parcel was a small white card with three words on it in Bill's handwriting — Bill's particular handwriting, which I had seen on the inside covers of folders four hundred times and had recognised from across rooms — and the three words were:

*For Richard — find her.*

I looked at the card for what was probably ninety seconds.

I looked at Margaret.

Margaret looked at the lemon tree.

She said, *he started preparing it for you in May. He had been working on it ever since.*

I said, *Margaret, I don't think I can.*

She said, *I know. He said you would say that. He said to tell you, the painting does not care whether you can. The painting only cares whether you will.*

I had not been able to say anything to that either.

She had stood up. She had set the parcel on my lap. She had bent down — she was a small woman, the top of her head came barely to my shoulder when we were both standing — and she had kissed me once on the top of my head the way a mother kisses a son she is about to send away, and she had said, *Richard. Go home. Open it tonight. Call me when you have read everything. Do not call until you have read everything.*

She had gone back inside the brown brick building. The screen door had closed behind her.

I had carried the parcel to the car. I had set it on the passenger seat. I had driven the four blocks home in silence with the parcel beside me. I had carried it up the stairs and into the front room of the small apartment on Cutting Boulevard where I had been sleeping since 2018, and I had set it on the dining room table that I had never used as a dining room table because I lived alone, and I had not opened it.

I had not opened it for forty-one hours.

I am telling you this because the rest of this book is what was in the parcel.

**CIRCA MONTPARNASSE, JANUARY 1920**

The studio on the rue de la Grande Chaumière is the size of a small parlour and twice as cold. The coal has not come this winter at all.

Jeanne is on the divan under three blankets and a coat that used to belong to Soutine. She is eight months and three weeks pregnant, and the doctor on the rue Lecourbe has told her, in a corridor, away from where her family can hear, that the child she is carrying may not arrive at all, because the woman who is carrying it has not eaten enough for two for three months, and the woman who is carrying it has been spitting blood into a handkerchief in the morning, and the doctor on the rue Lecourbe does not say the word out loud because he does not have to.

Modi is at the easel. He has been at the easel for six days. He is painting something nobody alive today has seen. He has not slept more than an hour at a stretch since the second week of December. He coughs into a rag every twenty minutes. The rag is not white any more.

Modi is thirty-five years old. He has thirteen days left.

He does not paint Kiki any more. He has not painted Kiki in two years because Kiki has gone on without him — she is nineteen, she is starting to be called Kiki by the new boy Mendjizky, she is sleeping on a different street, she has stopped coming to Chez Rosalie's because Chez Rosalie's has closed. He paints Jeanne now and Jeanne only. The canvas on the easel is of Jeanne, asleep on the divan in the same blanket and the same coat that she will be wearing when the doctor pronounces her dead three weeks later from a fifth-floor window in the rue Amyot, eight hours after Modi himself dies in the Hôpital de la Charité.

This is the chapter where they go.

He does not know yet.

She does not know yet.

Outside the rue de la Grande Chaumière the snow is starting again. It has been snowing in Paris for nine days. The Seine is iced over in the small inlet by the Pont de Sully. The cabs are not running. The Métro is running because the Métro is always running.

Modi paints for another hour. He paints Jeanne's hair. He paints it the way he has been painting her hair since 1917 — the great copper-red mass of it falling across her face, the line of her cheekbone underneath. He paints quickly because he is cold. He paints quickly because his right hand is starting to shake and he does not yet want to know what the shake means.

At nine o'clock in the evening he sets the brush down. He looks at the painting. He looks at her.

He says, *Jeanne.*

She opens her eyes. She has been awake the whole time. She has been pretending to sleep because Modi paints better when she pretends to sleep and they have both known this for two years and a half.

He says, *I am tired.*

She says, *come to bed.*

He does not come to bed. He goes to the corner of the studio where the bottle of cheap brandy is, the one the colour merchant gave him in lieu of a refund on bad cobalt blue, and he drinks half of it standing up. He comes back to the divan. He sits down on the floor at her feet. He puts his forehead against her knees through the three blankets.

She puts her hand on the top of his head the way she has put it there a thousand times in three years. Her hand has gone thinner. He can feel the bones through the skin.

He says, into the blankets, *Jeanne. The painting is finished.*

She says, *I know.*

He says, *It is the last one.*

She does not say anything.

He says, *I am sorry.*

She does not say anything for a long time. Then she says, *I know, my love.*

The snow has stopped falling outside. The room is very quiet.

I opened the parcel on the Thursday night.

I opened it after I had not opened it for forty-one hours, which was the longest forty-one hours of my life, and which I had spent driving up and down the 580 and the 80 in a small loop and twice stopping at a Vietnamese restaurant in El Cerrito that I had been eating at on Thursdays for nine years and that I cannot eat at any more.

Inside the parcel, in order from top to bottom:

A single typed letter, folded in three, on Bill's office letterhead. Six pages. Dated the seventeenth of May, 2025. Beginning: *Richard. If you are reading this, I am gone. I am sorry I did not get to finish this with you. I am writing this in the small hope that you will pick it up.*

A small leather-bound notebook, the kind a bookseller in Rome would call a *zibaldone,* about the size of my palm. Bill's handwriting on every page. Three hundred and forty pages. The first dated the second of October, 1986. The last dated the eleventh of October, 2025.

Three black plastic Xerox folders, each about an inch thick, marked in red Sharpie:
**1986–1999 / The First Search.**
**2000–2018 / The Wildenstein Period.**
**2019–2025 / Bratislava and After.**

A single photograph, black and white, four inches by six, of a small canvas propped against a chair in what looked like a sun-flooded studio with the windows open. The canvas in the photograph was clearly the painting. The studio in the photograph I did not recognise.

A second photograph, smaller, of a young woman seated on a chaise longue in what could only be the same studio. The photograph was dated, in pencil on the back, *Paris, 1917.* The young woman was Alice Prin.

A small white envelope marked, in Bill's handwriting on the front, *Richard — open this last.*

A larger manila envelope marked, in Bill's handwriting on the front, *The Names. Do not lose this.*

A single business card, printed on cream stock, the kind a Parisian framer would have used in the 1930s. The card had a name on it in copperplate engraving. The name was a Czech name. The card had a phone number on it. The phone number had the international prefix for Slovakia.

I read the first letter first. I read it three times.

Bill had not been a Modigliani specialist for forty years. Bill had been a Modigliani specialist for sixty-one years. Bill had inherited the search from a man named Aaron whose name was on the card.

Bill had not been looking for the painting because Bill had been the dealer. Bill had been looking for the painting because the painting had been in Bill's family. The painting had been in Bill's family since 1923. The painting had been removed from Bill's family in 1942 in Paris by men with armbands. The painting had passed through eleven sets of hands across eighty-three years before surfacing in a closet in Bratislava in 2013.

Bill had not been working on the painting for the seventy million dollars.

Bill had been working on the painting because the woman in the painting had once given Bill's great-uncle Aaron, who had been a tubercular twenty-eight-year-old colour-merchant's apprentice in Montparnasse in 1917, the only kindness of his short life.

I closed the letter.

I put it on the table.

I walked to the window of my apartment on Cutting Boulevard. I looked at the parking lot of the dental office across the street. The dental office sign read BAY AREA FAMILY DENTAL. The painted toothbrush on the front door was the same painted toothbrush I had walked past one hundred times on my way to the post office.

I had thought I had been a broker hired by an old man to sell a painting.

I had not been.

I had been a man named in a letter dated the seventeenth of May 2025 by a man whose great-uncle had once stood in a doorway in Montparnasse in 1917 and watched a sixteen-year-old waitress at Chez Rosalie's give him an apple she had stolen from a fruit stall on the rue du Montparnasse because he was hungry and could not afford one.

I went back to the table.

I opened the small white envelope last.

Inside, folded once, was a single sheet of paper. The paper was thin, the kind that European archives use. It had a single line on it, written in pencil, in a hand I would later learn to recognise as Aaron's hand.

The line read:

*Alice Prin gave me an apple on the eleventh of October 1917 at the corner of the rue du Montparnasse and the rue Vavin and I have not been the same since.*

The date below the line read 1968.

I sat at the table for a long time.

Then I picked up the phone and I dialled the Slovak number on the business card.

The man who answered, after seven rings, said, in heavily accented English, *yes?*

I said, *my name is Richard Triberg. Bill is gone. He told me to call you.*

There was a long pause. Then the man on the other end said, *Mr. Triberg. I have been expecting your call for nine years. Come to Bratislava.*

I went to Bratislava.

That is the chapter where Bill leaves and Modigliani leaves and Jeanne leaves on three separate January nights one hundred and four years apart, and what the chapter is finally about is not the leaving but the staying. Margaret stayed. Aaron stayed in the line on a piece of paper for sixty-eight years. Alice stayed in the painting for a hundred and ten. Bill stayed in the parcel on the dining room table on Cutting Boulevard until I opened it, and is, in a real sense, still in it. The painting itself stayed in a closet in Bratislava through two world wars and eight different owners and the partition of Czechoslovakia and the fall of the Berlin Wall, because the painting is patient in a way the people who chase it are not.

The book is the staying.

The painting is the proof.

The white card with the three words is the only sentence in the world that has ever obliged me to do something I knew, at the time I read it, I was going to spend the rest of my life trying to do.

*For Richard — find her.*

I have been trying.
