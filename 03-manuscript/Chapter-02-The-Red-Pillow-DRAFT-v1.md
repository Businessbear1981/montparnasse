# Chapter II — The Red Pillow

*Working draft, 29 May 2026. ~1,400 words. Proof-chapter for the fade-mechanism and the dual-timeline structure. Sourced from PART XVIII of the Exposé (pp 139–146) and Breakout Report 1 (the Tate's catalogue entry on Nevinson's* An Artist Studio in Montparnasse).

---

If you really want to know about it, I found her the second time on a Tuesday afternoon in a museum gift shop, which I had not gone into a museum gift shop on purpose since I was twelve years old, and I am only telling you that now because the rest of what follows requires you to understand I was not looking. People who write books like this always say they were not looking. I really wasn't.

It was a postcard. The Tate had a stack of them on a wire rack by the door, the kind of rack that wobbles when you spin it. The postcard was of a painting called *An Artist Studio in Montparnasse,* by a British painter I had heard of exactly once — Christopher Richard Wynne Nevinson. C. R. W. The painting itself had hung in the Tate, the postcard said, since 1927. It had been donated by H. G. Wells, of all people, who I did not know had collected paintings, but apparently he did, and apparently he had given this one away.

I bought the postcard for one pound forty. I did not know yet why.

What I knew was that there was a red pillow on the couch in the background of the painting, and the red pillow looked exactly like the red pillow on the couch in the painting Bill had spent the last three years of his life trying to authenticate. The red pillow with the greenish tassel trim, the one Modigliani had painted under his sitter in 1917. The one in the small canvas — forty centimetres by thirty-three — that was at that moment in a back room in Bratislava under a velvet cloth.

You could not see the painting on the Nevinson postcard. You could see the studio it had been painted in.

I went back to my hotel. I called Bill. I told him I thought I had just found Modigliani's 1917 studio in a Tate postcard for one pound forty.

Bill was quiet for a long time. Then he said, *come home.*

That was when the second part of my life began. I just did not know it yet.

**CIRCA MONTPARNASSE, AUTUMN 1917**

The studio is on the rue de la Grande Chaumière, on the third floor, with two tall windows that face north because that is the side of the building you rent if you want to paint and you cannot afford coal. The windows are open even in October, because there is no coal. The two artists who share the studio have an arrangement: Nevinson takes the morning light, Modigliani takes the afternoon. The afternoon is what the small canvas was painted in.

The red velvet pillow is on the couch by the wall. It has been on the couch since the lease was signed in the spring of 1916. Nobody is sure where it came from. Soutine claims he brought it. Brancusi claims he brought it. Modigliani, who pays his rent in drawings, does not claim anything; Modigliani's lungs are bad and Modigliani has stopped claiming anything that involves talking more than necessary.

The white shawl is draped over the chair where Modigliani's hat usually goes. The white shawl will appear in two dozen of his 1917 nudes — including the small canvas. The green hat is on the chair beside the shawl. The green hat belongs to a girl who is at this moment standing in the doorway of the studio with rain in her hair, because the rain has come on suddenly the way the rain comes on in Paris in October.

Her name is Alice Prin. She is sixteen years old. She is going to be called Kiki two years later by Henri Broca at a cabaret on the Boulevard Edgar Quinet, but tonight she is still Alice and she is wet and she is laughing.

Sisley Huddleston is sitting in the corner. Huddleston is a journalist for the *Christian Science Monitor* and the New York *Herald Tribune* and he writes literary letters for a London weekly that pays him in pounds. Huddleston is here because Huddleston is writing a piece about Montparnasse for an American magazine and Nevinson has agreed to be sketched while he paints, which is a small vanity that Nevinson has not yet thought through. Huddleston has his notebook open. He has not yet drawn anything. He is watching Alice.

Modigliani turns from the easel.

He does not say anything to her. He never says anything to her in any of the accounts. He just lifts his chin slightly, the way a man does when he wants someone to sit, and points at the couch with the red pillow on it.

Alice walks to the couch. She does not look at Huddleston. She does not look at Nevinson, who has stopped painting. She puts the green hat on the chair beside the white shawl. She lies down on her side. She folds her hands below her left knee, the way she has learned to fold them in the rooms of men who do not deserve her, and she looks at Modigliani, and Modigliani begins to paint.

Huddleston, in the corner, finally writes something in his notebook. He will remember the sentence and reuse it nine years later, in the letter he writes to the Paris edition of the *New York Herald Tribune* on the twenty-fourth of February, 1927, the letter that will accuse Christopher Richard Wynne Nevinson of altering the painting before he gave it to the Tate.

The sentence Huddleston wrote, that autumn afternoon in 1917, was: *the girl on the couch.*

The girl is on the couch. The pillow is red. The shawl is white. The artist on the bench is bearded and Italian and is going to be dead before he is thirty-six. The painting Modigliani is making — the small one, the forty by thirty-three — will outlive everyone in the room and end up in a back room in Bratislava ninety-five years from now, and the painting Nevinson is making, the larger one, of the studio they are all in, will hang in the Tate for a hundred years before anyone realises whose studio it was.

Modigliani paints her hands first. He always paints the hands first. He paints them folded below her knee. He paints them carefully. He is painting them as the gesture of a girl giving herself back to herself in front of a man who has not come, this time, to take anything from her.

Outside the window the rain is letting up. Inside the studio nobody speaks.

✱ ✱ ✱

I flew home the next morning. I did not sleep on the flight. I had the Nevinson postcard in my breast pocket and I kept taking it out and looking at it, the way a man looks at a photograph of someone he has been told he is going to meet. The red pillow was right there in the corner of the painting. I did not know yet that I would spend the next two years of my life proving that the bearded artist was Modigliani, and that the nude who had been in the original sketch — the nude who Huddleston had argued about in the letter to the *Herald Tribune* — had been removed from the painting before it was donated, and then painted back in, standing this time instead of reclining, by Nevinson himself in a panic about what the Leicester Galleries would say if they realised what the painting actually showed.

I did not know any of that yet. I just had the postcard.

Bill met me at the airport. He looked at the postcard for thirty seconds and put it in his pocket and said, *let's go to my office.*

We went to his office. That was the beginning of the second year. The second year is what this chapter is about.
