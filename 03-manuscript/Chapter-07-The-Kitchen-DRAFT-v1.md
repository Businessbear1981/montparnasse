# Chapter VII — The Kitchen

*Working draft v1, 29 May 2026. ~2,200 words. CIRCA MONTPARNASSE, a Tuesday night in November 1925. Kiki in the apartment at 31 bis rue Campagne-Première that she shares with Man Ray. After midnight. The boys come in one at a time. She is cooking. The chapter the reader falls in love in.*

*The chapter answers Sean's question — how does the audience fall in love with her — by letting them stand in the kitchen for an hour and a half and watch what she does without trying. The five frames braid through: she is the siren (the room comes to the song), the throne (it organises around the stove), the crown (she will wear it twenty-eight more years and die from it), Peter (she leads them and feeds them), and the dull comparison (in her orbit they are reaching). No frame is named. The scene is just a Tuesday night.*

*Five Lost Boys come through the door before sunrise: Soutine, Foujita, Mendjizky, Pascin, Man Ray. Four of them will be dead before Kiki. One of them — Pascin — will hang himself in his studio four years and seven months from this exact night. The reader does not know this. The chapter does not say it. The chapter only lets the reader be in the room.*

---

**CIRCA MONTPARNASSE, NIGHT OF TUESDAY 10 NOVEMBER 1925**

The apartment at 31 *bis* rue Campagne-Première is on the third floor, above a courtyard, behind a green door that does not lock properly because the landlord has not fixed it since the war. The kitchen is two metres wide. A coal stove against the back wall, a small zinc table, three chairs that do not match. A window over the courtyard that does not close all the way in winter. A single electric bulb on a brass chain that Man Ray has rigged to swing on a small pulley over the table because he likes the way the light moves when it swings.

It is twenty minutes past midnight on a Tuesday.

Kiki is at the stove.

She has been at the stove for forty minutes. There is a beef cheek in a copper pot that she bought at three this afternoon at the boucherie on the rue de Vaugirard from a butcher who lets her have the cuts the rich do not know about for the price the rich would pay for soup bones. There is a half-bottle of red wine she has been pouring into the pot every ten minutes from a glass she has also been drinking from. There are pearl onions she peeled with her fingers because the small knife is missing. There are carrots in the second pot. There is a quarter of a baguette on the table that she will use to soak the sauce, eventually, when the boys arrive, which they will, because tonight is Tuesday and on Tuesday they all know where she is.

She is wearing a man's white shirt that is too big for her and a pair of black stockings and nothing else. The shirt is Man Ray's. The stockings she bought herself. The bulb swings slowly above her on the brass chain. Her hair is bobbed. There is a smudge of red wine on the side of her left hand from when she missed the pot.

She is humming under her breath. The tune is a song her grandmother used to sing on Tuesdays in Châtillon when the bread was rising and the kitchen was empty. The song is filthy.

The green door downstairs opens.

A pair of unmatched footsteps comes up the three flights, the way footsteps come up three flights when the person taking them has been in the rue Delambre since seven o'clock and on his second bottle of whatever the Dingo bar was pouring tonight. The door of the apartment opens without a knock. Soutine comes in.

Chaim Soutine is thirty-one years old. He is the unhappiest man in Paris. He has a face like a wet animal. He has been in France for twelve years and is still, when he is drunk, more comfortable in Yiddish. He has on a brown coat that he has not taken off in nine days. There is paint on the collar. He stands in the doorway of the kitchen and looks at Kiki at the stove and says nothing.

She does not turn from the stove.

She says, *Chaim. Sit. The chair on the left is not broken.*

He sits in the chair on the left, which is not broken. He puts his head down on his arms on the table. After a minute he says, into the table, *Kiki, I painted a chicken today.*

She says, *was it any good.*

He says, *no.*

She says, *eat the bread.*

He breaks off a corner of the baguette without lifting his head and eats it without looking up. She pours a little more wine into the pot. The bulb swings.

The green door downstairs opens again.

This time it is Foujita, who comes up the stairs the way Foujita always comes up the stairs, which is silently, because Foujita walks the way a cat walks. He has on the round wire-rimmed glasses and the small black moustache and the close-cropped hair with the fringe across the forehead that he will be wearing for the next forty-three years, until he dies in 1968 in the chapel he is going to build himself in Reims. Tonight he is thirty-nine. He has a long box under his arm wrapped in brown paper and tied with twine. He sets the box on the corner of the kitchen table and stands behind Soutine's chair and looks at Kiki.

She turns from the stove for the first time since she heard him on the stairs. She looks at the box. She looks at him.

She says, *Léonard. What.*

He says, *it is for you. It is not finished. Look later. Not now.*

She says, *if it is for me you have to tell me what is in it.*

He says, *it is a drawing of you in the bath last week.*

She says, *the one where you said the light was wrong.*

He says, *I fixed the light.*

She turns back to the stove. She says, over her shoulder, *Léonard. Open the wine. The good one. From the Berthe Weill people. Behind the door.*

He goes behind the door, where the case of wine is, the case the gallery sent her for the painting she gave them last month. He pulls a bottle. He opens it with the corkscrew that lives in the windowsill because the drawer is broken. He pours four glasses. He sets one beside Soutine, who has not lifted his head; one for himself; one for Kiki at the stove; and a fourth, on the corner of the table, for whoever comes next. Then he sits down opposite Soutine and lights a cigarette and says nothing.

It is twenty past one in the morning.

The green door opens for the third time.

The footsteps are heavy and careful, the footsteps of a man who is too drunk to be careful and is being careful anyway. Mendjizky. Maurice. The boy who named her, six years ago, in his bed in a small room in the rue Joseph-Bara when she was nineteen and he was twenty-eight and he was the first man she had ever slept with on purpose. They lived together for three years. They have not lived together for two. He comes to the kitchen at midnight on Tuesdays the way the rest of them do because the rest of them do and because he does not know what else to do, and Kiki cooks for him the way she cooks for the rest of them, and they do not talk about it.

He stands in the doorway. He has on the grey suit Pascin gave him last Christmas because Mendjizky cannot afford a suit and Pascin can. He has been at the Dôme since nine. He has not eaten.

He looks at Kiki at the stove.

She says, without turning, *Maurice. There is a chair.*

He sits in the chair beside Foujita. He picks up the fourth glass of wine and drinks half of it in one swallow. He sets the glass down. He says nothing.

Foujita slides his cigarettes across the table to him. Mendjizky lights one. The bulb swings.

It is ten minutes to two.

The fourth man does not come up the stairs. The fourth man rings the bell from the courtyard, because the fourth man is Pascin and Pascin is the only one who still rings the bell. Kiki hears it. She wipes her hand on the side of her stocking and goes to the window and pushes it open and leans out and looks down into the courtyard and says, *Jules. The door is open. The door is always open.* And she pulls a long copper key out from inside the front of the shirt — Man Ray's shirt — and tosses it down into the courtyard, and Pascin catches it without looking up because Pascin has caught the key in the courtyard sixty times this year, and he unlocks the green door he could have pushed, and comes up the three flights with a small canvas tucked under his left arm.

He comes into the kitchen with the canvas. He does not say hello. He sets the canvas against the wall by the table, facing the wall, the painted side hidden. He sits in the third chair, which is in fact the only matching chair, between Foujita and Soutine. He nods at Mendjizky. He pours himself the last glass of wine. He looks at Kiki.

She is still at the stove.

She says, *Jules. Show me.*

He stands up. He turns the canvas around. He sets it against the wall facing into the room and steps back so the bulb can swing over it.

The canvas is forty centimetres by thirty. Oil. A woman on a bed. The bed is mostly white. The woman is mostly white and rose. Her head is turned slightly toward the viewer and her eyes are open but not focused. Her left hand is across her stomach. Her right hand is dropped over the edge of the bed and the light is going down her fingers the way the light goes down a child's hand when the child has fallen asleep over the side of a chair.

The room is silent.

Soutine lifts his head off the table.

Mendjizky stops with the cigarette halfway to his mouth.

Foujita removes his glasses.

Kiki takes the wooden spoon out of the pot and lays it across the top of the pot. She wipes her right hand on the side of her thigh. She walks the four steps from the stove to the canvas. She crouches down in front of the canvas in her bare feet and the man's white shirt. She looks at the painting for what Pascin tells his dealer Léopold Zborowski the following Friday was *three seconds.*

She stands up.

She turns back to the stove and picks up the wooden spoon and dips it into the sauce and tastes it. She adds a pinch of salt. She stirs once.

Without turning, she says, *Jules. Yes. It works.*

Pascin closes his eyes.

He stands beside the canvas for a long moment. Then he reaches down and picks the canvas up and turns it back to the wall, gently, the way you turn a sleeping child onto its side. He sits down at the table and pours himself a glass from the bottle Foujita had opened earlier, which is the lesser wine. He drinks half of it. He does not look up.

It is twenty past two.

The green door opens for the fifth and last time.

Man Ray comes in last because Man Ray always comes in last. He has been in the darkroom in the studio on the rue Bréa since seven. He has a print folded in his coat pocket. He nods at the room. He goes behind Kiki at the stove. He puts his right hand on the small of her back. He looks over her shoulder into the pot. He says, *how long.*

She says, *fifteen.*

He nods. He takes his hand off her back. He sits down at the table in the chair Foujita has just vacated, because Foujita has stood up to lean against the door frame and smoke. The bulb swings.

Kiki, at the stove, says — without turning, without raising her voice — *à table. Ten minutes. Maurice, the bowls are above the sink.*

Mendjizky stands up. He goes to the cupboard above the sink and takes down five bowls. He sets them on the table. He sits back down.

It is two-thirty in the morning of Wednesday the eleventh of November 1925.

Kiki, at the stove in Man Ray's white shirt, hums the song her grandmother used to sing on Tuesdays in the kitchen in Châtillon-sur-Seine when the bread was rising and the house was empty.

By the second verse, the kitchen is laughing.

By the third verse, Pascin is laughing too, into his sleeve.

By the fourth verse, Soutine — who has not laughed in six weeks — has lifted his head off the table to listen.

By the fifth verse, she is dancing slightly at the stove, in the man's white shirt and the black stockings, with the wooden spoon as a microphone, the way her grandmother used to dance in the kitchen alone on Tuesdays in 1909 when she did not know Alice was watching.

The bulb swings.

It is later than two-thirty.

Nobody in the kitchen is looking at the bulb. Nobody in the kitchen is looking at the canvas against the wall. Nobody in the kitchen is looking at the door. Everyone in the kitchen is looking at Kiki.

She is not looking at any of them. She is looking at the stew.

That is the room they spent their whole lives trying to find their way back to.

*[End of Chapter VII. Pascin will hang himself on the second of June 1930, four years and seven months from this night, in a studio on the rue Joseph-Bara that is two streets from the apartment where he had once given Mendjizky the grey suit. Soutine will die in occupied Paris on the ninth of August 1943 of an untreated stomach ulcer in the back of an ambulance trying to reach a hospital that will not take him because he is Jewish. Mendjizky will live until 1951 and his catalogue after 1925 will be lighter than the six oils he made of her between 1919 and 1922. Foujita will outlive her. Man Ray will outlive her by twenty-three years and spend most of those years photographing women who are not her.*

*The reader does not know any of this in Chapter VII. The reader knows only that on a Tuesday night in November 1925, in a two-metre-wide kitchen above a courtyard on the rue Campagne-Première, a woman cooked a beef cheek for the five men who loved her, and one of them set a canvas against the wall, and she looked at it for three seconds, and said yes.*

*The book is the cost of that yes.]*
