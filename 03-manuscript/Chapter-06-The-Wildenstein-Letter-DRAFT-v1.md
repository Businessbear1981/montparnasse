# Chapter VI — The Wildenstein Letter, 2012

*Working draft v1, 30 May 2026. ~5,400 words. Richard's first trip to the Institut Restellini lab in Geneva, January 2025, two months after Bratislava. Camille hands him a folder Marc Restellini had been keeping in a separate archive — Marc's own 2012 Wildenstein notes on the painting, with Bill's later annotations in the margin. Richard reads it. Then he says out loud, for the first time in four years, what he has not been admitting. The chapter slips into 1924 — the kitchen at 31 bis rue Campagne-Première, Man Ray about to make Le Violon d'Ingres, Pascin laughing, Mendjizky in the grey suit, Hemingway two tables over with Hadley, Anaïs Nin in the doorway watching. A verbatim page of Kiki's 1929 memoir at the centre. Closes on Richard back in his hotel room in Geneva reading the same page from her memoir she lived through.*

*All seventeen frames braided. Salinger voice in Richard's modern arc. Tone mutations in the historical: Pascin's tender-tired register when he speaks, Hemingway's Pernod register at the next table, Man Ray's Self-Portrait voice when he handles the Leica, the Pernod named on every café surface. Three Lost Boys carry through (Pascin, Mendjizky, Man Ray) by recurring detail.*

---

If you really want to know about it I flew to Geneva four months after Bratislava because Camille Aubry had sent me an email at six o'clock on a Tuesday morning Geneva time that said exactly one sentence: *Mr. Triberg. There is a folder Marc had been keeping that you should read in person. Come when you can.* I had been waiting four months for any email from Camille that was longer than one sentence, and when it finally was longer than one sentence and the additional sentence was *come when you can,* I bought the ticket from a payphone in a Walgreens in El Cerrito because the Walgreens was the closest payphone to the apartment on Cutting Boulevard and the apartment had become, somewhere in those four months, a place I did not want to do anything in that involved planning a thing.

The flight was Lufthansa, SFO to Frankfurt to Geneva. I had been on Lufthansa twice in three months by then and the cabin crew on the Frankfurt-Geneva connection was beginning to recognise me, which is the kind of thing that happens to a man who is taking his life apart by air-mile across the back half of his fifties.

I want to tell you something I have not yet told you and that is going to make the next six chapters make sense, and I am telling you now because if I told you in the first chapter you would have closed the book.

I am in love with her.

I am a fifty-two-year-old American art broker in North Richmond, California. She has been dead since 1953. I have seen her face in a photograph by Julian Mandel, in a photograph by Man Ray, in the small canvas in a wooden crate the size of a hatbox that I had carried from Bratislava and that was, on the morning I got the email from Camille, still on the dining-room table of my apartment in Richmond.

I know how it sounds. I know it is the kind of thing the year 2026 has decided men are not allowed to admit to any more without an apology behind it. I am not apologising. I am also not in any particular pain about it, if you want to know the truth. The opposite, in fact. Being absurdly in love with a French queen who died before my parents met has been, in a way I did not see coming, the best four years of my life. I have flown to Bratislava at three days' notice. I have stood in a Geneva Freeport vault under a Mission Order signed by Marc Restellini. I have written two hundred and twenty-eight pages that ten years ago I would have laughed at any man for writing.

Bill warned me. He said, *Richard, the painting does not care whether you can. The painting only cares whether you will.* He should have warned me about the woman in the painting. He did not have to. *She had warned me herself, in 1917, by sitting on a red velvet pillow in a small studio on the rue de la Grande Chaumière for a dying Italian painter, and waiting one hundred and nine years for the next man to be foolish enough to look at her seriously.* I am the next man. I am okay with it. I think you should be, too.

That is the thing I had been avoiding saying for four years and that I am saying now, in the middle of the book, so the rest of the book reads in the right key.

The flight got into Geneva at eleven in the morning. Camille met the connection at Cointrin. She had on a charcoal-grey wool coat over plain dark clothes and a thin steel chain with her keycard around her neck and she did not smile when she saw me at the bottom of the escalator. She did not smile when she saw me on any of the seven other occasions across the next eighteen months. *Camille's resting face is not impatience; I had taken six weeks the first time to figure that out.* Camille's resting face is just Camille's face when she is thinking, which is most of the time.

She said, *Richard.*

I said, *Camille.*

She said, *the folder is at the lab. Get in the car.*

We drove to the Geneva Freeport on the rue du Stand. Camille drove. I have never seen Camille drive faster than the speed limit and I have never seen her drive a centimetre slower than it either. She is, like the chromatographs she runs, exact.

The Geneva Freeport is a complex of grey buildings two blocks from the river that holds, according to the *Financial Times,* somewhere between forty and a hundred billion dollars' worth of fine art at any given time. *Nobody knows the actual number because the freeport is designed so that nobody knows the actual number.* The Restellini lab is in basement two of building three.

Camille's bench is in a room with no windows and the kind of pale fluorescent overhead light that does not exist in nature. The chromatograph she runs is the size of an upright piano and is made of the kind of stainless steel that costs a hundred and fifty thousand dollars even before you account for the chromatography. The room smells of nothing because Camille has had the air handled into smelling of nothing.

She did not take me to her bench. She took me to a small reading room down the corridor, the kind of room a lawyer would have in a city where lawyers are paid in francs. She unlocked the door with her keycard. She closed the door behind us. She gestured at the small table in the middle of the room and said, *sit.*

I sat.

There was a folder on the table. It was a manila folder, the kind I had been seeing in Bill's office for three years. *That was the first thing I noticed about it. Bill's exact kind of folder.* The folder was thick. It had a small white label on the front in handwriting I had not seen before. The handwriting was Marc Restellini's.

The label read: *Modigliani — Wildenstein, 2012 — pending second visit.*

Camille said, *you have been wondering for two months how Marc could possibly have spent two months in twenty-twelve with this painting and not finished. That folder is the answer. He finished. He finished privately. He did not file it with Wildenstein. He kept it.*

I said, *Camille.*

She said, *yes.*

I said, *did Bill know about this folder?*

She said, *Bill knew it existed. Bill did not know what was in it. Marc would not show it to him.*

I said, *why is Marc letting me see it now?*

She said, *Marc has not seen the canvas yet. Marc will see the canvas the week after next, the Tuesday after you fly back. He thought you should have this in your hand before then. He did not say why. I think he thinks you have earned it. I am not Marc, I do not speak for Marc. I am telling you what is in front of you.*

She did not sit down. She stood with her back to the closed door and her hands in the pockets of her coat. She watched me. *She is not someone who watches an ordinary thing. She watches the way a chromatograph watches.*

I opened the folder.

The first page was a typed report on Wildenstein letterhead, dated the seventeenth of November, 2012. The report was Marc Restellini's. The report was six pages. The report was about a small canvas, oil on canvas, forty point five by thirty-three centimetres, signed in black paint in the lower right *modigliani,* of a seated nude on a red velvet pillow with her hands folded below her left knee.

The report's conclusion was: *probably authentic — pigment, composition, signature, and biographical period all consistent — but documentation insufficient to enter the catalogue raisonné at this time. Recommendation: hold the work in private circulation until further documentation surfaces. Note: the sitter, in this curator's view, is almost certainly Alice Prin (1901–1953), commonly known as Kiki de Montparnasse, age sixteen at the date of painting.*

I read the conclusion three times.

The third time I read it I realised something I had not realised until that moment, which was that Marc Restellini had identified the sitter as Kiki *in twenty-twelve.* The whole 110-years-lost narrative that Bill and I had been writing for three years had been quietly written by Marc Restellini in a private folder in 2012 and never released. Marc had known. Marc had been waiting for the documentation.

Bill had been preparing the documentation.

Camille watched me figure this out.

When I had figured it out I looked up at her and I said, *Bill knew Marc had identified her.*

Camille said, *yes.*

I said, *Bill spent the last fourteen years of his life building the documentation Marc had said was missing.*

Camille said, *yes.*

I said, *Bill never told me.*

Camille said, *Bill did not need to tell you. Bill needed you to figure it out at exactly the moment you were going to figure it out, which was today, in this room, with this folder in your hand. He had timed it.*

I started turning pages in the folder.

The next four pages were Marc's technical notes. The Coquillé lead-white was identified at the first sample point. The cobalt was identified. The red lake was identified. *Marc had done the chemistry Camille was about to repeat with better instruments thirteen years later.* Marc had also done a stylistic comparison with three signed Modi nudes of known 1917 provenance and noted that the brushwork in the upper-right tablecloth was identical to the brushwork in the upper-right tablecloth of *Nu Couché aux Bras Levés.* Marc had attached three small black-and-white reproductions of the comparison paintings, paper-clipped to the page.

The fifth page was a clean typed line down the centre of the page that read, in Marc's hand under it in black ink: *Sitter — Alice Prin (Kiki). Confirmed. — M.R., 17/XI/2012.*

The sixth page was a single sentence in Bill's handwriting in the margin in pencil. Bill had been in this folder at some point — Marc had let him in for an afternoon some year in the mid-2010s — and Bill had written a single sentence in the margin in pencil. Marc had not erased it. Marc had kept it.

The sentence read: *This is the year she became Kiki — but not yet to the world. M.R. knows. We wait. — W., 2014.*

That was it. That was the folder. Six pages, one signed sentence, one pencil annotation, and forty-three additional sheets of comparative technical data I was not yet qualified to read.

I sat back in the chair.

I sat for what Camille later told me was eleven minutes.

After eleven minutes I said, *Camille.*

She said, *yes.*

I said, *did you know what was in the folder before today?*

She said, *yes.*

I said, *for how long?*

She said, *for fourteen months. Marc gave it to me to inventory in November twenty twenty-three. I read it once and I closed it and I put it in the locked archive and I waited for Marc to tell me what he wanted me to do with it.*

I said, *Camille. Why do you do what you do.*

She said, *the chromatograph.*

I said, *that is not the whole answer.*

She said, *no. It is not. The other half is that I am French and I was raised by women who told me that France had once been the home of three or four women who had built a country inside the country and that the country inside the country had been better than the country outside it. My grandmother knew Anaïs Nin a little. My great-aunt knew Lee Miller. They told me the painters in those years had been giants but the women had been the country, and that the country had been small and short and that when it had ended the painters had spent the rest of their lives trying to find their way back to it. I came into chemistry because I am not Anaïs Nin and I am not Lee Miller and I am not Kiki, but I can tell you, in a chromatograph, what year a piece of lead-white was made in, and that is a way of letting one of those women back into the room.*

I had not heard Camille say a paragraph that long in eight months of working with her.

I did not say anything.

After a while she said, *Mr. Triberg. May I tell you something I should not.*

I said, *yes.*

She said, *Marc is going to authenticate the painting.*

I said, *how do you know.*

She said, *because Marc kept the folder for fourteen years and did not file it, and Marc does not keep folders for fourteen years unless Marc is waiting for somebody to bring him the rest of the file. You are the rest of the file. The two-hundred-and-twenty-eight pages are the rest of the file. The Bratislava paperwork is the rest of the file. He will not say so before he has handled the canvas. But he is going to authenticate.*

I said, *Camille.*

She said, *yes.*

I said, *I have been in love with her for four years.*

She looked at me for a long time.

She said, *I know, Richard. We all do.*

She opened the door of the small reading room.

She said, *the lab closes at six. I am going to leave the folder with you for the afternoon. The receptionist will let you out. There is a café across the street called the Café du Soleil. They have a Saint-Joseph by the glass. I will be there at half past six.*

She closed the door behind her.

**CIRCA MONTPARNASSE, FRIDAY 13 JUNE 1924**

The kitchen at 31 *bis* rue Campagne-Première is small and the window over the courtyard does not close all the way and there is a candle on the table that is the only light because the electric bulb went out three weeks ago and Man Ray has not yet got around to replacing it. The candle is in a brass holder that used to belong to her grandmother in Châtillon and that she has carried with her every time she has moved since 1913. The candle is the only thing in the room that has been with her since before Paris.

She has been at the stove for thirty-five minutes. The pot on the stove is the same copper pot. She has put two onions and a piece of ham and a bottle of cheap red and a bone the butcher on the rue de Vaugirard let her have for nothing into it, and the room smells of the ham and the red and the candle. She has eaten nothing since the bread she had at the Dôme this morning. She is wearing the man's white shirt that is too big for her — Man Ray's — and the same black stockings, and her hair has the cut she had Foujita give her last week with a pair of barber's scissors in his apartment because she had decided to be done with the length. She is twenty-two years old. She is the same height she was when she stepped off the train at the Gare de Lyon at twelve and she will be the same height the day she dies in 1953 — *one metre forty-eight, the smallest woman in any room she will ever walk into,* and you would not know it from any of the photographs because the photographs lie about that and she does not care.

The door downstairs opens.

She hears the door from the kitchen because the door is right under the kitchen window and she has been listening for the door for an hour. She knows whose step it is on the staircase before he is at the top. *He has been climbing those stairs every Friday for two and a half years and his step has been the same step every Friday and she would know it on a different staircase in a different city.*

The footsteps are Jules Pascin's.

The door of the apartment opens without a knock.

Pascin comes in.

He is thirty-nine years old. He has on the brown wool coat he has had since 1918 that is the colour of nothing in particular and that has paint on the right cuff that he has not bothered to remove since the spring. He has a small parcel under his left arm wrapped in butcher's paper and tied with twine. He has been at the Dôme for two hours already and he has had four Pernods at the Dôme and he is not drunk because Pascin does not get drunk in any measurable sense. He simply has a different number of Pernods inside him than the day requires.

He comes into the kitchen. He puts the parcel on the kitchen table. He looks at her at the stove. He says, in the slow tired voice that is his voice when he has been at the Dôme for two hours and has not yet had a fight, *Kiki, my love. I have brought you something.*

She does not turn from the stove. She says, *what.*

He says, *open it.*

She says, *I am cooking.*

He says, *I will hold the spoon.*

She turns. She gives him the wooden spoon. He stands at the stove the way she had been standing at it. She walks the four steps to the table. She unties the twine. She unfolds the butcher's paper.

Inside is a small canvas, eighteen centimetres by twenty-four. Oil. A woman lying on a divan in a slip. The woman is not her. The woman has red hair instead of black. The woman has a different mouth. The woman is somebody Pascin slept with in March of this year for two weeks and that he has not slept with since. The painting is beautiful. The painting is the best canvas Pascin has made this year.

She looks at it for the length of two breaths.

She says, *Jules. It is good.*

He says, *I painted it for you.*

She says, *the woman is not me.*

He says, *no. The woman is not you. But I painted it for you. I have not been able to paint anyone but you for two years and a half and last Tuesday I sat down at the easel and I painted a different woman and when I had finished I knew it was a painting for you because I would not have been able to paint a different woman if you had not given me the permission to.*

She looks at him at the stove holding her spoon.

She does not say anything for a long time.

Then she says, *Jules. Bring it to the table when we eat. I will hang it on the wall by the candle. I will not give it away.*

He says, *thank you.*

He says it in the voice of a man who has been waiting all afternoon to be told that.

She walks back to the stove. She takes the spoon back from him. He stands beside her at the stove for a long moment with his hand on the small of her back through the man's white shirt. She does not say anything. He does not say anything either. The candle in the brass holder makes the kitchen a small warm room.

Then he goes to the table and sits down at the chair that is his chair and lights a cigarette and waits for the others.

The downstairs door opens again twelve minutes later.

This time it is Maurice Mendjizky in the grey suit Pascin gave him at Christmas two years ago. Mendjizky is thirty-four and has been her lover from 1919 to 1921 and has not been her lover since and is one of the men who comes to her apartment on a Friday evening anyway. He sits down at the table beside Pascin and he does not say hello to either of them because Mendjizky does not say hello on Fridays. He takes a Pernod from the bottle Pascin has opened and he pours it the way a man pours a Pernod that he does not want to think about pouring. He drinks half of it standing up before he sits down.

Mendjizky's voice, when he speaks, is the voice of a Polish man who has been in Paris seventeen years and who still mutters in Yiddish to himself at the stove when he thinks no one is listening. Tonight he says one sentence and the one sentence is, *Kiki. Foujita wants to know if you are coming to the Sélect at midnight.*

She says, *yes.*

He says, *I will tell him.*

That is all he says all evening.

The downstairs door opens a third time at twenty minutes past eight. The third arrival is Foujita with a small Japanese watercolour box he has been carrying under his coat for three streets and he does not show her what is in it. He sits down in the third chair quietly. He takes the green tea from his coat pocket that he carries everywhere because he does not drink Pernod most nights. He brews it in a small cup using the kettle Man Ray has not bothered to clean since March. He does not speak. He watches her at the stove. *He watches the way a man watches an animal at the edge of a meadow.*

The fourth arrival is Man Ray.

Man Ray comes in last because Man Ray comes in last on every Friday she has been with him. He has been in the darkroom on the rue Bréa since two in the afternoon. He has a contact print folded in the inside pocket of his coat. He does not show her the print. He puts his hand briefly on the small of her back at the stove the way Pascin had done an hour earlier and the touch is not the same touch. *The Pascin hand had been the hand of a man asking permission. The Man Ray hand is the hand of a man who has decided he no longer needs to ask.* She does not turn from the stove.

He sits down at the table. He lights a cigarette. He says, *Kiki.*

She says, *Man.*

He says, *how long.*

She says, *fifteen.*

He nods. He pours himself a Pernod from Pascin's bottle. He does not speak again until she serves the stew.

*And this is the part of the evening I cannot give you in the third person any longer,* because the part I want you to see is what *she* sees when she turns from the stove with the wooden spoon in her right hand and looks at the four of them around the small kitchen table — *Pascin at the head with the cigarette he has not yet drawn on, Mendjizky on his left with the empty Pernod glass, Foujita on the right with the green tea in the small Japanese cup, Man Ray at the foot of the table with his back almost to her — and she sees, not the four men, but four pieces of her heart, on loan,* sitting at the table waiting to be fed.

She turns back to the stove. She lifts the lid of the copper pot. She tastes the sauce with the wooden spoon. She adds a pinch of salt.

She says, into the pot, *à table.*

The men get up.

In her doorway behind them, where she has been standing for the last forty minutes without any of them noticing her, is Anaïs Nin. Nin is twenty-one years old. She is six months younger than Kiki. She had come up the stairs with Foujita at eight twenty and she had not come into the kitchen because she had not wanted to interrupt. *She had wanted to watch.* She has been watching the way a woman watches another woman doing the thing that woman has not yet learned to do, and that she will spend the next forty years of her own writing trying to learn.

Nin steps into the kitchen.

She says, in French, *Kiki. May I sit with you tonight.*

Kiki looks at her.

She says, *Anaïs. The fifth bowl is on the shelf above the sink.*

Anaïs Nin takes the fifth bowl down off the shelf above the sink. She sits down at the kitchen table in the chair that is the chair that nobody had been sitting in. She is the first woman who has eaten at this table on a Friday in eight months and she will write about it, in the diary she has been keeping since she was eleven, in the entry dated *13 juin 1924 — j'ai mangé chez Kiki ce soir et j'ai vu pour la première fois ce qu'est une femme qui n'a demandé à personne la permission d'être ce qu'elle est.*

The candle in the brass holder makes the kitchen a small warm room.

Outside on the rue Campagne-Première, two streets away on the corner at the Dôme, a tall American at a corner table is drinking a Pernod with his first wife who has just got off the boat-train from Le Havre with a baby they cannot afford and a small leather suitcase that holds everything they own in the world, and he is six weeks from meeting Kiki at La Rotonde for the first time at the kind of party Anaïs Nin will not yet have been invited to. *He does not know yet about the woman two streets away at the small kitchen with the candle.* He will know about her in six weeks. He will write the foreword four years from now. The sentence he will write in the foreword is already starting to form in his head four years before he has the room for it.

The four men at her table eat the stew.

Pascin says, into his bowl, *it is good, Kiki.*

She says, *it is the same stew it was last Friday, Jules.*

He says, *that is why it is good.*

She does not smile. She does not need to. The room sees the not-smiling and the room understands it.

I read the folder for three hours.

I left the Geneva Freeport at quarter to six. I walked to the Café du Soleil. I sat at the corner table by the window. I ordered the Saint-Joseph by the glass. I drank the first glass slowly. The second glass arrived without my asking for it. I drank the second one slowly too. Camille arrived at half past six exactly. She was wearing the same charcoal-grey coat over the same plain dark clothes and she sat down at the small marble-topped table across from me and she said, *Richard. How are you.*

I said, *Camille. I am okay.*

She said, *what did Bill have written on the sixth page in pencil.*

I said, *"This is the year she became Kiki — but not yet to the world. M.R. knows. We wait. W., 2014."*

She said, *Bill was a romantic.*

I said, *Bill was the most romantic man I have ever met.*

She said, *and you are the second.*

I drank some of the Saint-Joseph.

I said, *Camille. Bill knew Marc had identified her in 2012. Bill spent fourteen years building Marc the documentation. Bill died eight weeks before Marc was going to see the canvas again. Bill never got to be in the room.*

She said, *I know.*

I said, *I am going to be in the room for him.*

She said, *yes.*

I said, *will you be in the room with me.*

She said, *yes, Richard. I will be in the room with you. I am the one who runs the chromatograph.*

I had not really been asking about the room.

She knew that.

She drank her glass. She did not say anything for a long time. *I had spent the four months since Bratislava learning that Camille's silences were a kind of language and that the longer ones were the ones you read the most carefully.* This one was about twenty seconds and what it said was, *Richard. We will all be in the room. Including the woman in the painting. The painting is not just the canvas any more, you understand? When Marc sees it, in two weeks' time, he is not just going to see the canvas. He is going to see Bill's fourteen years and your two hundred and twenty-eight pages and the Bratislava paperwork and the parcel that your friend's widow gave you in the courtyard and the chromatograph I have not yet run and the woman the painting is of. He is going to authenticate all of it. Not just the canvas. All of it.*

She did not say any of that.

She said, *another Saint-Joseph?*

I said, *yes.*

The waiter brought another Saint-Joseph.

I went back to my hotel at eleven. I did not sleep. I lay on the bed in the hotel room on the rue de la Servette in Geneva with the window open and the air cold and the small reading lamp on, and I read the page Camille had given me. It was a photocopy of a single page from Kiki's 1929 memoir, in the original French. The page she had marked was the page about the kitchen at 31 *bis* rue Campagne-Première on the Friday nights in 1924. *I had not known until that afternoon that Kiki had written about the kitchen herself.* I read the page three times.

I read it again the next morning on the flight back.

I am going to give you the page now, in her translation, because the way she wrote the kitchen is what the book is finally about, and I am not going to paraphrase it because nobody can paraphrase Kiki and I am not going to try.

> *On Fridays in those days we ate at our small kitchen on the rue Campagne-Première, the men I lived around and I, around a wooden table with one candle that had been my grandmother's because the electric bulb had gone out and Man would not change it. Pascin came at seven. Mendjizky at eight. Foujita at twenty past eight, always with green tea. Man at the end because he had been in the darkroom all afternoon. I made a stew with whatever the butcher on the rue Vaugirard had given me that week for nothing because the butcher liked me and the cheese man liked me and the wine man liked me and the bread man liked me, which is the only reason any of us ate at all in those years. The stew was always the same and the men always said it was the best stew in Paris. The men were lying about the stew but the men were not lying about anything else they said at that table. We ate. We drank. We smoked. We laughed about whatever the room had laughed at that afternoon at the Dôme. We did not talk about money because we did not have any. We did not talk about the future because none of us had thought of one. The window above the courtyard would not close and the night air came in and the candle would gutter and we would push the candle into the wax and it would go again and we would finish the wine and one of us would say it was time for the Sélect and we would all go to the Sélect because going to the Sélect was the same as going to the kitchen except that the Sélect had more chairs. We did not know then that those Fridays were what we would all spend the rest of our lives trying to find our way back to. We did not know we were inside what was going to be the only thing we would ever have been inside. We thought we were having dinner. We were having Montparnasse.*

I read it on the plane back to San Francisco with the lights down in the cabin and the man in the seat beside me asleep and the small overhead light on, and I read it three more times.

I cried twice on the flight. The first time was in her paragraph about the candle that had been her grandmother's. The second time was the last sentence. *We thought we were having dinner. We were having Montparnasse.*

When I got home Margaret picked up the phone on the second ring. She said, *Richard.*

I said, *Margaret. Marc is going to authenticate.*

She said, *I know.*

I said, *how do you know.*

She said, *Bill told me he would in 2014. I have been waiting.*

She hung up.

I sat at the kitchen table in my apartment on Cutting Boulevard for the rest of the night. The crate from Bratislava was still on the table. The parcel was still beside it. The white card with the three words was still propped against the lemon-tree photograph.

*For Richard — find her.*

I had been finding her for four years and I had not found her until that afternoon in a small reading room in the basement of the Geneva Freeport, and I had not finished finding her either, because finding her was the work of the rest of my life, and I had figured that out somewhere over the Atlantic at thirty-five thousand feet with the small overhead light on and the man in the seat beside me asleep.

I went to bed at quarter to four in the morning.

I slept.

For the first time in eight months I slept.

*The grandmother's candle. The white shirt that is not hers. Anaïs Nin at the doorway. The Pernod on every table. Hemingway two streets away with Hadley and the boat-train. The kitchen as Wonderland in miniature. Her heart at the table being eaten in four bowls. The last sentence of her memoir on the page in her own voice. Richard reading it in a hotel room in Geneva at one in the morning. All seventeen frames present, none named.*
