# Kiki-Centric Voice Discipline

*Locked 30 May 2026. The companion to FRAMES.md and the Master Timeline. This document corrects the drift Sean caught: the historical chapters are reading like a cast of giants with Kiki implied, when the book is supposed to be Kiki at the centre and the giants in her orbit. **She is the sun. The book has to keep showing her.**

---

## The single test

Every paragraph in every Circa Montparnasse vignette asks one question before it is allowed to stand:

> **Whose interiority are we in?**

If the answer is *a Lost Boy's,* the paragraph is wrong. **Rewrite until the answer is Kiki's.**

The Lost Boys are allowed to *appear* — to walk in, to growl, to bring a canvas, to drop a coat. They are not allowed to *think* on the page. Only she thinks on the page. The men are objects in her room. She is the subject.

---

## The seven kinds of paragraphs allowed in a historical chapter

1. **Her body** — where she is sitting/standing/walking, what she is wearing, what she has eaten today (or hasn't), what her hands are doing, whether she is cold.
2. **Her eye** — what she is looking at, what she notices, what she does not notice, the small detail of the room she catches.
3. **Her ear** — what she hears, whose voice in the room, what she chooses to listen to, what she tunes out.
4. **Her want** — what she is reaching for in the moment. Bread. A cigarette. To be left alone. To be looked at.
5. **Her memory** — what the present moment surfaces in her from before (Châtillon, the apple-tree orchard, the schoolmaster's ruler, the grandmother's bread Tuesday). The book uses memory to keep her continuous across chapters.
6. **Her silence** — what she does not say. What she chooses not to give the room. The men talk; she watches them talk and decides what to keep.
7. **Her decision** — the small actions that she takes that the room reads as nothing and that change what comes next. Lays a hand on a coat. Sings the wrong song. Walks out without finishing the wine.

Anything else is the wrong paragraph.

---

## What the Lost Boys are allowed to do on the page

- **Walk in.** Through a door, through a kitchen, through a café. Visible to her.
- **Speak — briefly.** One line, sometimes two. Reported as dialogue, not as monologue.
- **Be looked at by her.** She catalogues their bodies, their coats, their tics, with the precise observational discipline she has had since age seven in the orchard.
- **Bring something.** A canvas. A bottle. A grief. The thing they bring is the thing she will decide about.
- **Leave.** The way they leave (a coat, a hat, a glance back) is hers to register.

What the Lost Boys are **not** allowed to do on the page:

- Have an interior monologue.
- Carry the chapter's emotional weight.
- Get a backstory paragraph that does not pass through her.
- Be described in the third-person omniscient *they will eventually do X* time-travelling voice. (That voice is reserved for the narrator addressing the reader, not for unpacking the Lost Boys.)

## One demonstration — Chapter II, the existing paragraph

### What is on the page now (Modi-centric)

> *Modi is at the easel with a brush in his right hand. He is thirty-one. He has been coughing for about a year now. He has bought the lead white on credit because he has bought everything on credit since the spring. The canvas in front of him is small — forty by thirty-three — and the ground is wet because he laid it down three days ago and is impatient. The signature is going to go in the lower right corner. He has not put it there yet.*

The reader is in Modi's head. He is the subject of every sentence. *He* is at the easel. *He* is thirty-one. *He* has been coughing. *He* has bought lead white on credit.

### What it should be (Kiki-centric)

> *She has been in the room thirty seconds. She has taken off the coat. She has hung it over the arm of the divan beside the green hat. She has watched the rain on the back of her left hand for the count of three before she has dried it on the side of the shirt that is not hers. She looks at the man at the easel. He is thirty-one and he has been coughing into a rag for a year and the rag is on the floor by his foot and she does not need to be told what colour the rag is. He has not looked back at her yet. The brush is in his right hand. The canvas is small. The ground is wet. He laid the ground three days ago. She knows because she walked past the studio on the Sunday and the window was open and she had seen him standing at the canvas through the glass. She had not gone in. She had walked back to the rue Joseph-Bara. She is here today because she chose to be here today. He will not put the signature in the lower right corner for another four hours and she will be gone by then.*

She is the subject of every sentence. The reader is in her body, her eye, her ear, her memory of Sunday, her choice. *Modi exists in this paragraph entirely through what she notices about him.* He is the object. She is the sun.

The same paragraph carries the same documented facts — the brush, the cough, the lead-white-on-credit (cut), the wet ground, the small canvas, the lower-right signature. **What changes is the centre of gravity.** Everything happens to and through her.

## Discipline rules for braiding (cumulative with FRAMES.md and the Master Timeline)

1. Before writing a Circa Montparnasse paragraph, name out loud (or in a sidebar comment): *Whose interiority?* If the answer is anyone but Kiki, do not write the paragraph yet.
2. The Lost Boys are introduced through her perception, not their biographies. *"He is the man Mendjizky brought to her table three weeks ago,"* not *"Maurice Mendjizky was a Polish painter who arrived in Paris in 1907."*
3. Her body gets the page. Her hands. Her hair. Her feet. What she has eaten today. Whether she is cold. *The reader is in her body before any sentence about anything else.*
4. **Memory binds the chapters.** The grandmother's bread Tuesday returns in the kitchen 1925. The chimney shadow on the rue de la Grande Chaumière in 1920 returns in Geneva in 2026 when Camille shows Richard a photograph of the same studio. The apple at the corner of the rue du Montparnasse and the rue Vavin returns in Bratislava when Richard reads Aaron's pencil line. Memory keeps her continuous across one hundred and ten years.
5. **The men are the orbit. She is the sun.** When the paragraph ends, the reader should feel her gravity, not their motion.
