# Prologue — A Girl Too Big for the Tree

*Draft v1, 29 May 2026. ~2,100 words. Châtillon-sur-Seine, 1908 to 1913. Alice from seven to twelve. The legend's beginning, before Paris, before Modi, before the queen, before any of it. Fitzgerald wallpaper, present tense, third-person omniscient. No Richard. No fades. The whole prologue lives in one voice and one place, and ends on the train to Paris.*

---

**CHÂTILLON-SUR-SEINE, AUTUMN 1908**

She is seven years old. She is climbing a tree.

The tree is in the orchard behind her grandmother's house in Châtillon-sur-Seine, in the Côte d'Or, in the third week of October. The apple she is climbing for is in the highest branch on the south side. There are easier apples lower down. She is not climbing for those.

Her grandmother is at the bottom of the tree calling up to her in a voice that has stopped sounding angry an hour ago and has started sounding tired. Alice, the grandmother is saying. Alice. Alice. The grandmother is sixty-four years old. The grandmother has been raising her since she was three months old, when Alice's mother had gone to Paris with a man called Henri who was not Alice's father, although nobody is sure anymore who was. The grandmother's name is Marie-Augustine. The grandmother smells of the lavender she keeps in the linen press and of the cooking smoke that lives in her hair on Tuesdays because Tuesday is the day she does the bread.

Alice gets the apple.

She comes down the tree the way she came up. She tears her dress on a branch she did not see, on the back of the left shoulder, in a line that is going to take her grandmother an hour and twenty minutes to repair that evening by the kerosene lamp at the kitchen table. She lands on the ground with both feet and her dress torn and the apple in her hand and the apple is exactly as red as she had thought it would be from below, and she gives it to her grandmother, and her grandmother does not eat it.

Her grandmother says, *Alice, tu seras ma mort.*

Alice does not understand it that way. Alice thinks her grandmother is saying she is the most important thing in her grandmother's life. Alice grins and runs into the house. She is going to come back outside in twenty minutes with a piece of bread and find her grandmother sitting on the wooden bench by the back door with the apple uneaten in her lap, looking at the orchard the way an old woman looks at an orchard when she has just told her granddaughter for the seventh time that she will be the end of her.

The apple is still uneaten in the morning. The grandmother gives it to one of the chickens.

**CHÂTILLON-SUR-SEINE, SPRING 1909**

The schoolmaster is a man named Pelletier who used to teach in Lyon and who came back to Châtillon-sur-Seine three years ago to bury his mother and never left. Pelletier teaches the village children in a single room with three rows of benches and a stove that does not draw properly. Pelletier has a wooden ruler with a brass edge on it. Pelletier uses the ruler on the children's hands.

Alice is in the front row because the front row is where the children who cannot sit still are put. Alice has been talking to the boy beside her about a thing she saw in the river yesterday — a fish, possibly a trout, possibly larger than her arm — and Pelletier comes down the aisle and stands over her and tells her to hold out her hand. Alice holds out her hand. Pelletier brings the ruler down across her palm.

Alice bites him.

She bites him on the wrist of his right hand, the hand that holds the ruler, hard enough that she draws blood. Pelletier drops the ruler. Pelletier looks at his wrist for a long second and then at Alice. The other children are silent. Outside the window a single bird is calling. Alice looks at Pelletier and does not look away.

Pelletier walks her home himself.

Her grandmother opens the door. Pelletier explains. Alice's grandmother thanks Pelletier, in the way old women thank schoolmasters who walk their granddaughters home, and closes the door, and then turns and looks at Alice for a long time. Alice waits.

Her grandmother says, *Alice. You will be the end of me.*

Alice says, *Grand-mère, the ruler hurts.*

Her grandmother sits down at the kitchen table. Her grandmother puts her face in her hands. After a minute her grandmother says, *I know it does.*

Alice climbs into her grandmother's lap. They sit at the table together for a long time and neither of them speaks. Alice does not go back to Pelletier's school. The grandmother teaches Alice the alphabet at the kitchen table with a piece of chalk on a slate that used to belong to Alice's mother, in the evenings after the bread is done, and Alice learns to read in six weeks. The grandmother does not tell anyone in the village how fast.

**CHÂTILLON-SUR-SEINE, MIDSUMMER 1910**

She is nine. She is in the market square fighting a boy named Léon Brossard, who is eleven, and who has called her a name she had not heard before but knew immediately, by the way he said it, was a name she would have to make him take back. Léon is taller than she is. Léon has older brothers who have taught him how to hold his fists in front of his face. Alice has nothing of the kind. Alice has only that she will not stop until he sits down on the cobblestones and says he is sorry, and Léon does not yet know that about her.

The fight lasts six minutes. The fight is watched by every market-day vendor on the east side of the square, by three nuns from the convent who have just come out of the cheese shop with their basket, and by the mayor's wife, who will tell the story at the dinner table that night, and through her at the prefect's office at Dijon by the end of the week, by which time the story will have grown by a foot in either direction.

Léon sits down on the cobblestones.

He says he is sorry.

Alice walks home. She is bleeding from a cut on her lower lip and from a scrape on her right knee. Her hair has come out of its ribbon. There is a piece of cabbage leaf in her hair from when she had pushed Léon into the vegetable cart on the third minute. Her grandmother is in the front yard waiting because the news has reached the front yard before Alice has.

Her grandmother says nothing.

Her grandmother takes her into the kitchen. Her grandmother fills a basin with cool water from the pump. Her grandmother cleans Alice's lip and her knee. Her grandmother takes the cabbage out of her hair. Her grandmother brushes her hair out and ties a new ribbon. Her grandmother does not say *Alice, you will be the end of me,* because the grandmother has come to understand, this summer, that what she had been calling the end of her was something closer to the opposite, although she does not have the words for it.

The grandmother says, *child, you are too big for this village.*

Alice does not know what that means yet.

She is going to find out.

**CHÂTILLON-SUR-SEINE, EARLY AUTUMN 1913**

The letter from Paris arrives in the second week of September.

The grandmother reads it twice at the kitchen table by the lamp. The grandmother folds it carefully along the creases the mother had made. The grandmother puts the letter on the table between them. The grandmother looks at Alice across the table.

Alice is twelve.

She has grown four centimetres this summer. She has begun to sing in the evenings at the village fountain, not because anyone has taught her to but because she has discovered the sound of her own voice in the dusk and has decided it pleases her, and the old women in the square have begun coming out of their kitchens to listen, and the old women have begun saying, that child, that child. Alice has discovered that the old women's *that child* and her grandmother's *Alice, you will be the end of me* are different versions of the same sentence, and Alice has discovered that she likes hearing both.

The grandmother says, *Alice. Your mother has written.*

Alice waits.

The grandmother says, *your mother is settled now. Your mother has a small flat. Your mother wants you to come to her in Paris.*

Alice looks at the letter on the table between them. She does not pick it up. After a moment she looks back at her grandmother.

Her grandmother is crying without making any sound.

Alice gets up from the table. Alice goes around the table. Alice climbs into her grandmother's lap, the way she has been climbing into her grandmother's lap since she was four years old, although she is too big for it now and they both know it. Alice puts her arms around her grandmother. Her grandmother holds her.

Her grandmother says, *I have known this letter was coming since the day she left. I have known it for twelve years.*

Alice says, *Grand-mère, will I come back?*

Her grandmother does not answer for a long time.

Then her grandmother says, *I do not know, my heart. I do not think so.*

**THE TRAIN, OCTOBER 1913**

She is on the morning train from Dijon to the Gare de Lyon. She has a small leather bag her grandmother packed for her. The bag contains two dresses, a pair of shoes, a slate, a small pile of letters from her mother that the grandmother has kept in a tin in the linen press, and an apple from the orchard.

She is wearing the brown dress that has the embroidered collar her grandmother made her for her tenth birthday. Her hair is in two braids. She has a piece of paper in her left coat pocket with her mother's address in Paris on it. The address is on the rue Dauphine in the sixth arrondissement, which means nothing to her because she has never been to Paris and has never opened a map of Paris and does not know yet what an arrondissement is.

The train goes through wheat fields. The train goes through small towns. The train goes through Sens. The train goes through Fontainebleau. The train enters Paris through tunnels and warehouses and the back walls of factories. The train comes into the Gare de Lyon at twenty minutes past one in the afternoon.

She steps down onto the platform. She has never seen so many people in one place in her life.

She is twelve years old. She is one metre forty-eight centimetres tall. She is wearing the brown dress with the embroidered collar. Her hair is in two braids. She is holding the small leather bag with both hands.

She is, on this Tuesday afternoon at the Gare de Lyon, on the twenty-first of October in the year 1913, the largest thing in Paris.

Paris is not going to know what to do with her either.
