# Prologue — A Girl Too Big for the Tree

*Working draft v2, 29 May 2026. ~2,800 words. Six sequences from Châtillon-sur-Seine 1908 to the Gare de Lyon 1913. Adds the new third sequence — the letters in the linen press, the girl reading them by lamplight, building Paris in her head, deciding she will be the one men listen to in the cafés. The fed-up grandmother holds the whole Prologue. The queen is born in a Burgundy farmhouse twelve years before the Jockey Club.*

---

**CHÂTILLON-SUR-SEINE, AUTUMN 1908**

She is seven years old. She is climbing a tree.

The tree is in the orchard behind her grandmother's house in Châtillon-sur-Seine, in the Côte d'Or, in the third week of October. The apple she is climbing for is in the highest branch on the south side. There are easier apples lower down. She is not climbing for those.

Her grandmother is at the bottom of the tree calling up to her in a voice that has stopped sounding angry an hour ago and has started sounding tired. *Alice,* the grandmother is saying. *Alice. Alice.* The grandmother is sixty-four years old. The grandmother has been raising her since she was three months old, when Alice's mother had gone to Paris with a man called Henri who was not Alice's father, although nobody is sure anymore who was. The grandmother's name is Marie-Augustine. The grandmother smells of the lavender she keeps in the linen press and of the cooking smoke that lives in her hair on Tuesdays because Tuesday is the day she does the bread.

Alice gets the apple.

She comes down the tree the way she came up. She tears her dress on a branch she did not see, on the back of the left shoulder, in a line that is going to take her grandmother an hour and twenty minutes to repair that evening by the kerosene lamp at the kitchen table. She lands on the ground with both feet and her dress torn and the apple in her hand and the apple is exactly as red as she had thought it would be from below, and she gives it to her grandmother, and her grandmother does not eat it.

Her grandmother says, *Alice, tu seras ma mort.*

Alice does not understand it that way. Alice thinks her grandmother is saying she is the most important thing in her grandmother's life. Alice grins and runs into the house. She is going to come back outside in twenty minutes with a piece of bread and find her grandmother sitting on the wooden bench by the back door with the apple uneaten in her lap, looking at the orchard the way an old woman looks at an orchard when she has just told her granddaughter for the seventh time that she will be the end of her.

The apple is still uneaten in the morning. The grandmother gives it to one of the chickens.

**CHÂTILLON-SUR-SEINE, SPRING 1909**

The schoolmaster Pelletier has a wooden ruler with a brass edge. Pelletier uses the ruler on the children's hands. Alice has been talking to the boy beside her about a thing she saw in the river yesterday — a fish, possibly a trout, possibly larger than her arm — and Pelletier comes down the aisle and stands over her and tells her to hold out her hand. She holds it out. He brings the ruler down across her palm.

She bites him on the wrist of his right hand, the hand that holds the ruler, hard enough that she draws blood.

Pelletier walks her home himself.

Her grandmother opens the door. Pelletier explains. Her grandmother thanks Pelletier, in the way old women thank schoolmasters who walk their granddaughters home, and closes the door. She turns to look at Alice.

Her grandmother says, *Alice. You will be the end of me.*

Alice says, *Grand-mère, the ruler hurts.*

Her grandmother sits down at the kitchen table and puts her face in her hands. After a long minute she says, *I know it does.*

Alice climbs into her grandmother's lap. They sit at the table together for a long time. Alice does not go back to Pelletier's school. The grandmother teaches her the alphabet on a slate at the kitchen table, in the evenings after the bread is done, by the same kerosene lamp she had repaired the dress under the autumn before. Alice learns to read in six weeks.

The grandmother does not yet know what reading is about to do.

**CHÂTILLON-SUR-SEINE, WINTER 1911**

The linen press stands against the north wall of the front room. It is made of cherrywood. It has belonged to Alice's grandmother since 1872, when her grandfather built it for her as a wedding present. The press has three shelves. The top shelf holds the linen. The middle shelf holds the wool. The bottom shelf holds, behind a stack of folded woollen underclothes that have not been touched in eight years, a small tin box that used to hold *bonbons à l'anis* and now holds the letters that Alice's mother has sent from Paris.

Alice is ten years old.

She has discovered the tin.

She does not yet know how she discovered it. She knows that on a Wednesday afternoon in November, when the grandmother was at the village washhouse and Alice was supposed to be sweeping, she was instead lifting the woollens, one by one, off the bottom shelf of the press, and that the tin had been waiting there as if it had always known that one of them would come for it eventually.

There are forty-two letters in the tin. They are bound by a length of red ribbon. The handwriting on the envelopes is loose and slanted and uneven, the handwriting of a woman who learned to write quickly and not particularly well. The earliest letter is dated April 1902, six months after Alice was born, and was sent from a print shop on the rue Vaugirard. The most recent is from this past August.

Alice puts the woollens back exactly the way she found them. She takes the tin to her room. She slides it under the corner of her mattress, under the corner that is closest to the wall, where the grandmother has not put a hand in eight years.

She begins reading the letters that night.

She reads them by the kerosene lamp on the small wooden table under her window, after the grandmother has gone to bed. She reads them in order. She does not skip ahead. She is going to be three months getting to the bottom of the pile because she is going to read each letter four and five times before she lets herself go on to the next one.

The letters are not interesting if you are an adult. Her mother writes about the print shop. The smell of the ink. The long hours setting type at the linotype machine. A man called Henri who is kind to her. A boarder upstairs who plays the accordion at night. The cost of bread on the rue Cler. A woman in the apartment across the courtyard who beats a rug at the same hour every Sunday morning. A horse that fell in the rue de Sèvres in the snow last January and could not be got up.

To Alice the letters are the door to the next world.

She reads about the boulevards. She reads about the gas lamps and how at dusk the lamplighter comes along with a brass pole and lights them one by one and the whole street comes on the way her mother says — *as if someone had turned a key in the back of the city.* She reads about a café on a corner where a fiddler plays on Sunday afternoons and the women come out of the church across the square and stop, on their way home, to listen. She reads about a woman she has seen at the ballet wearing a hat with three feathers in it. She reads about a singer in a smoky room on the rue des Martyrs called Yvette Guilbert who can hold a single note for as long as a man can hold his breath and who, when she does, makes the whole room hold its breath with her.

Alice has not seen any of these things. Alice has seen the village fountain at sundown, the church at six in the morning when the grandmother goes for the bread, and the orchard, and the river, and the road that goes south to Dijon and that she has never been allowed past the second milestone of.

She invents the rest from the letters.

She invents Paris in her bedroom by the lamp at the small wooden table. She invents the gas lamps. She invents the lamplighter. She invents the smell of the ink at the print shop, which she imagines must be like the smell of the soil after rain, only bitter, only black. She invents the woman with the three feathers. She invents Yvette Guilbert in the smoky room. She decides that Yvette Guilbert is wearing a black dress and that the men at the tables are not breathing, the way her mother says they are not breathing, the way she has decided men do not breathe when the right woman is singing.

She decides she is going to be the woman the men do not breathe for.

She does not yet have a word for what she is going to be. She does not say *star* because the word *star* is not the word a girl in Châtillon in 1911 uses. She says *la femme qu'on regarde.* The woman one looks at.

She practises in the orchard. She practises in the hayloft of the barn behind the house. She sings the songs her grandmother sings on Tuesdays when the bread is rising and the house is empty. She sings *Les Filles de Camaret,* which is the filthy sailors' song her grandmother sings under her breath when she does not know Alice is listening and which the older women in Châtillon would not let their daughters in the kitchen for. Alice sings it to the cows because the cows are the only audience the barn loft can manage. The cows do not stop chewing. Alice tells herself this is not the cows' fault. This is the room.

The grandmother knows about the letters by Christmas.

The grandmother has known since the first Wednesday, when Alice put the woollens back not quite the way she found them, on the third fold instead of the fourth. The grandmother says nothing. The grandmother could take the tin back. The grandmother could move it to a place Alice could not find. The grandmother could burn the letters and lie about it.

The grandmother does none of those things.

The grandmother understands, sometime that winter, what the letters are about to do. The grandmother understands that what is being built in the room under her, by the lamp at the small wooden table, with her own bread-day song coming out of the loft of the barn behind the house in the afternoons, is the girl who is going to leave her.

The grandmother does not stop it.

When she catches Alice with the tin open on her bed one Sunday in March of 1912, she does not take it. She stands in the doorway. She watches Alice for a long moment. She says only, *put the woollens back exactly the way you find them. I will not fold them for you.*

Then she closes the door.

That is the closest thing to a blessing the grandmother is able to give her.

**CHÂTILLON-SUR-SEINE, MIDSUMMER 1910**

She is nine. She is in the market square fighting a boy named Léon Brossard, who is eleven, and who has called her a name she had not heard before but knew immediately, by the way he said it, was a name she would have to make him take back. Léon is taller. Léon has older brothers who have taught him how to hold his fists.

The fight lasts six minutes.

Léon sits down on the cobblestones. He says he is sorry.

Alice walks home bleeding from a cut on her lower lip and a scrape on her right knee and with a piece of cabbage leaf in her hair from when she had pushed Léon into the vegetable cart on the third minute. Her grandmother is in the front yard waiting because the news has reached the front yard before Alice has.

Her grandmother says nothing.

Her grandmother takes her into the kitchen. Her grandmother fills the basin with cool water from the pump. Her grandmother cleans her lip and her knee. Her grandmother takes the cabbage out of her hair and brushes it out and ties a new ribbon. Her grandmother does not say *Alice, you will be the end of me,* because the grandmother has come to understand that what she had been calling the end of her was something closer to the opposite.

Her grandmother says, *child. You are too big for this village.*

Alice does not know what that means yet.

She is going to find out.

**CHÂTILLON-SUR-SEINE, EARLY AUTUMN 1913**

The letter from Paris arrives in the second week of September.

The grandmother reads it twice at the kitchen table by the lamp. She folds it carefully along the creases the mother had made. She puts the letter on the table between them.

Alice is twelve.

The grandmother says, *Alice. Your mother has written.*

Alice waits.

The grandmother says, *your mother is settled now. Your mother has a small flat. Your mother wants you to come to her in Paris.*

Alice looks at the letter on the table between them. She does not pick it up. After a moment she looks back at her grandmother.

Her grandmother is crying without making any sound.

Alice gets up from the table. She goes around. She climbs into her grandmother's lap, the way she has been climbing into her grandmother's lap since she was four years old, although she is too big for it now and they both know it. She puts her arms around her grandmother. Her grandmother holds her.

Her grandmother says, *I have known this letter was coming since the day she left. I have known it for twelve years.*

Alice says, *Grand-mère, will I come back?*

Her grandmother does not answer for a long time. Then she says, *I do not know, my heart. I do not think so.*

Upstairs in Alice's room, under the corner of the mattress closest to the wall, is a tin that used to hold *bonbons à l'anis* and now holds forty-three letters bound with a red ribbon, the most recent of which is the letter on the kitchen table downstairs. Alice has read the new letter three times since Tuesday. She has not told her grandmother she has read it. She does not need to.

**THE TRAIN, OCTOBER 1913**

She is on the morning train from Dijon to the Gare de Lyon. She has a small leather bag her grandmother packed for her. The bag contains two dresses, a pair of shoes, a slate, the tin of forty-three letters from her mother — which the grandmother had wrapped in a clean linen cloth and placed at the bottom of the bag without a word, on the kitchen table, the morning Alice left — and an apple from the orchard.

She is wearing the brown dress that has the embroidered collar her grandmother made her for her tenth birthday. Her hair is in two braids. She has a piece of paper in her left coat pocket with her mother's address in Paris on it.

The train goes through wheat fields. The train goes through small towns. The train enters Paris through tunnels and warehouses and the back walls of factories. The train comes into the Gare de Lyon at twenty minutes past one in the afternoon.

She steps down onto the platform. She has never seen so many people in one place in her life.

She is twelve years old. She is one metre forty-eight centimetres tall. She is wearing the brown dress with the embroidered collar. Her hair is in two braids. She is holding the small leather bag with both hands.

She is, on this Tuesday afternoon at the Gare de Lyon, on the twenty-first of October in the year 1913, the largest thing in Paris.

Paris is not going to know what to do with her either.
